Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 12 Diurnal rescaling procedure

In the previous two chapters we introduced a new device and explored its meta and used it to collect diurnal and spatial data.

In this chapter we will define two approaches to recalculate sif for a give time of day ( for example Hyplant overpass). The first approach is based on an empirical fit of the change in SIF as a function of LAI found experimentaly. The method does not prescribe an a-priori shape of the diurnal cycle but rather finds the rate of change from observations at hand which makes it applicable only for interpolation, e.g. a dataset at target time is needed. Conversely, the second approach prescribes an a-priori shape of the diurnal cycle of SIF based on the diurnal PAR and thus eliminating the need for a reference measurement at target time.

12.1 The need for rescaling explained

One particular issue arising from any sampling procedure using a relocation system is the presence of a diurnal cycle in SIF. This is complicating comparison between measurements taken in different locations, since a moving system is by definition covering also the time. Hence, before comparing the steady state SIF from two locations the discrepancy in illumination conditions must be taken into account. In other words there must be a method to recalculate the SIF to a specific moment in time given auxiliary data.

One straightforward approach to deal with the discrepancy between measurement time is to calculate apparent fluorescence yield by normalizing SIF by the amount of incoming photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) at time of measurement. the resulting SIF yield estimates are usually in different physical units than the original directional spectral intensity in which SIF is measured. While for comparative analysis within a single study this is acceptable (e.g. for field phenotyping), for validation of remote sensing products, and for calculation of GPP (after the formulation in Gu2019), it is important to keep the standard units of the measurements.

Same but different wording

The presence of a distinct diurnal cycle in SIF is complicating comparison between measurements taken in different locations unless they are taken approximately at same time of the day. In other words, for TOC SIF measurements taken at two or more locations but on separate occasions one cannot distinguish if the difference in SIF is due to a structural/functional difference in plant status or due to a change in incoming radiation.

To circumvent such problem, very often, diurnal SIF measurements are normalized by amount of incoming photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) [cite]. Alongside accounting for the diurnal cycle, such approach has the additional benefit of providing a proxy estimate for photosynthesic efficiency - apparent SIF yield. However, a drawback of this approach is the use of wide variety of normalisation proxies (different proxies for PAR) to estimated SIF-yield which hinders standardization and comparability between studies. Furthermore, the resulting SIF yield estimates are usually in different physical units than the original directional spectral intensity [W.m2.sr-1.nm-1] in which SIF is measured.

12.2 Developing a rescaling approach based on empirical relationship between SIF and LAI

On August 2nd, Hyplant repeated the low altitude flight path performed a month earlier. During this overflight on the ground we repeated the spatiotemporal measurement procedure using the mobile FLOX with an one diference. One transect was sampled twice - in the morning and afternoon hours.

A closer look into the relative change in SIF from pre-noon into after-noon shows that such change is dependent on the LAI. Locations with higher LAI exhibit a greater increase in SIF during the day as those locations where less vegetation is present. 12.2.2 Empirical relationship

Based on the graph above, a theoretical representation of the diurnal course of SIF and the rate of change of SIF (\(\Delta F\)) in absolute units are shown in the figures below. The slope of the linear relationship between \(\Delta F\) and \(LAI\) is dependent on the time window, e.g. for a infinitely small time window the rate of change \(\Delta F\) will be zero for any \(LAI\).

Theoretical figures showing diurnal SIF and SIF vs. LAI for two different locations (LAI1, and LAI2) and three different times (to, tx, tNoon). First suffix denotes location (1 or 2), second suffix - time (o, x, Noon)

Knowing that for a given time window all lay on the slope we need two locations to express the slope of in the left graph above.

Thus the slope at time 0 is:

\[\begin{equation} t_0 = \frac{F_{1N} - F_{10}}{LAI_1} ; t_0 = \frac{F_{2N} - F_{20}}{LAI_2} \end{equation}\]

And the slope for time x is:

\[\begin{equation} t_x = \frac{F_{1N} - F_{1x}}{LAI_1} ; t_x = \frac{F_{2N} - F_{2x}}{LAI_2} \end{equation}\]

Then, solving for \(F_{1x}\) we have:

\[\begin{equation} F_{1x} = F_0 + \frac{LAI_1}{LAI_2} \cdot (F_{2x} - F_{20}) \end{equation}\]

12.3 Develping a-priori shape based on the quadratic fit on par

It is important to note that in the previous section the shape of the diurnal SIF was no known. Instead only the difference between time (o) and time (x) was considerted (\(\Delta SIF\). In this section we will prescribe a-priori the shape of SIF for one day and use this to The diurnal course of SIF can be expressed in terms of the Monteith LUE paradigm as:

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{t} = PAR_{t} .\Gamma.\phi F_{t} \end{equation}\]

Where \(\Gamma\) and \(\phi F_{t}\) are the structural and physiological componenets controling SIF. We will consider LAI as a proxy for \(\Gamma\) and \(\phi F_{t} = const\) and bulk them into one constant \(S = \Gamma.\phi F\)

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{t} = PAR_{t} \cdot S \end{equation}\]

The change in TOC SIF for an infinitely small time step \(\mathrm dt\) can be found by differentiating the above equation with respect to \(t\).

\[\begin{equation} \mathrm{d} SIF \mathrm{d} t = \mathrm{d} S \cdot PAR \mathrm{dt} \end{equation}\]

Since \(S=const\) it is clear that the change in SIF is only dependent on the difference in PAR.

\[\begin{equation} \mathrm{d} SIF \mathrm{d} t = S \cdot \mathrm{d}PAR \mathrm{dt} \end{equation}\]

Then finding SIF for any moment in time requires only one measurement of SIF and the rate of change in PAR. Considering that \(\mathrm{d} SIF \mathrm{d} t \equiv SIF_{t+1} - SIF_t\), the equation above can be rewriten as:

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{t+1} = SIF_t + S \cdot \Delta PAR \end{equation}\]

Even if the exact PAR reading is missing for the target time, we can approximate the daily course of PAR for a cloud free day in the mid latitudes using a quadratic function of the form:

\[\begin{equation} PAR_x = PAR_{max} \cdot (1 - x^2) \end{equation}\]

where \(PAR_{max}\) is the photosynthetically active radiation at solar noon, x is the time of the day in decimal units so that \(x \epsilon [-1;1]\) for sunrise and sunset, and \(x=0\) at solar noon.
