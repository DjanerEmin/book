Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 9 Exploring the influence of LAI and Chlorophyll content on TOC SIF using SCOPE simulation

Radiative transfer (RT) models are useful to explore the sensitivity and interdependence of SIF to various factors. In fact, extensive analysis based on the Sobol’s sensitivity criterion (Sobol 1990) revealed that TOC SIF is mainly controlled by LAI and Cab. Actually, these two, together with leaf architecture and the maximum potential carboxilation rate (Vcmax) explain up to 95% of the variations in total canopy fluorescence (J. Verrelst et al. 2015).

In the field, only two narrow bands from the total SIF emission are retrievable - the oxygen absorption bands(M. Meroni et al. 2009). These two estimates can be combined into a simple ratio, or an empirical formulation can be applied to get the total SIF in the range from 650 to 800. However, having two independent SIF estimates lets us define more complex transformations such as normalized indices or or other ratios. In this chapter we will simulate SIF for varying LAI and Cab using SCOPE and explore the sensitivity of SIF in the two retrieval bands to LAI, Cab, and standard vegetation indices. Then we will define several SIF indices and explore their sensitivity to the input LAI and Cab, and the vegetation indices.

SCOPE (Soil Canopy Observation, Photochemistry and Energy fluxes) is a vertical integrated radiative transfer and energy balance model linking visible and thermal infrared radiation to the heat and water fluxes from vegetation. A special routine is dedicated to calculating photosynthetic rate and chlorophyll fluorescence integrated over the whole canopy. The SCOPE model consist of separate cascading modules organized in: 1/ module for calculating incident solar radiation, 2/ module for calculating thermal radiation, 3/ energy balance module for latent and sensible heat flux, and 4/ a dedicated fluorescence module. The last, fluorescence module is based on the FluorSAIL model and calculates FTOC radiance spectrum from leaf level also accounting for canopy geometry. It gives the opportunity to control for canopy biophysical parameters and explore the sensitivity of the F signal to difference in leaf area and leaf chlorophyll levels (C. van der Tol et al. 2009).

9.1 Simulation

We used SCOPE (v.1.70) to simulate the effect of LAI and chlorophyll content on the hemispherical integrated reflectance and fluorescence for an unstressed C3 plant. To do so, we constrained all input parameters except LAI and Cab to their recommended default values (Table 9.1), while letting LAI to vary between 0.25 and 10 units, and Cab - between 10 and 100 g.cm-2. This resulted in a simulation with 760 permutations of possible LAI and Cab combinations. Fluorescence was estimated using the biophysical routine in (Collatz et al. 1991) and a mechanistic sub model based on a theoretical relationship (MD-12) described in (???). The simulation was performed using ARTMO software (v. 3.21)(???)

9.2 Results

From the full hemispherical fluorescence spectrum we extracted fluorescence for the two retrieval regions typically retrieved from hyperspectral data - fluorescence at 687 nm and 760 nm (Figure 9.1) and used these two values to calculate SIF indices.

Figure 9.1: Fluorescence output for a range of LAI and Cab values from SCOPE simulation.

The output reflectance was used to calculate VI following the definitions in Rascher (2015) and Badgley (2017) and Haboubane (2002). NIRv was calculated using reflectance at 800 nm and not radiance.

Below are the definitions for the different SIF estimates and the traditional and newly developed vegetation indices and a descriptive data table (Table 9.2):

SIF indices

  • SIF ratio: \(SIFr = \frac{F_{687}}{F_{760}}\)

  • SIF total: \(SIFtot = F_{760} + F_{687}\)

  • SIF index: \(SIFi = SIFtot*SIFr\)

  • SIF difference: \(SIFdif = F_{760} - F_{687}\)

  • SIF index2: \(SIFi2 = SIFdif*SIFr\)

  • SIF normalised difference: \(ndSIF = \frac{F_{760} - F_{687}}{F_{760} + F_{687}}\)

Vegetation indices

  • Normalized Difference Vegetation Index: \(NDVI = \frac{R_{800} - R_{670}}{R_{800} + R_{670}}\)

  • Near-infrared Corrected NDVI: \(NIRv = NDVI . R_{800}\)

  • Simple Ratio Vegetation Index: \(SR = R_{800}/R_{670}\)

  • Enhanced Vegetation Index: \(EVI = 2.5\cdot \frac{R_{800}-R_{670}}{R_{800}+6\cdot R_{670} + 7.5\cdot R_{480}+1 }\)

  • Triangular Chlorophyll Absorption Index: \(TCARI = 3 \cdot (R_{700} - R_{670}) - 0.2 \cdot (R_{700} - R_{550}) \cdot \frac{R_{700}}{R_{670}}\)

  • Optimized Soil Adjusted Vegetation Index: \(OSAVI = (1+0.16) \cdot \frac{R_{800}-R_{670}}{R_{800} + R_{670} + 0.16}\)

  • TCARI/OSAVI

9.2.1 SIF as function of LAI and Cab

The two SIF estimates retrievable by remote sensing data () show non-linear relationship with LAI and Cab (figure ??). For far-red fluorescence the shape of the curve is positive while for red fluorescence - it is positive vs. LAI and negative vs. Cab. Thus, LAI leads to increase in total SIF emittance but saturates at high canopy densities. Chlorophyll also leads to increase in emitted SIF 760 while it leads to decrease in in SIF687 due to reabsorption.

Figure 9.3: SIF in the two retrieval regions as a function of LAI and chlorophyll content

A four parameter model can describe the combined effect of LAI and Cab on the SIF 760. The model has the following shape:

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{760} = (p_1 \cdot (1 - e^{-p_2 \cdot C_{ab}}) + p_3) \cdot (1- e^{-(p_4 - 0.1*ln(Cab)) \ \cdot LAI^{0.9})}) \end{equation}\]

9.2.2 SIF as a function of VI

Most of the vegetation indices have a saturating curve in relation to LAI. As was shown in (Haboudane et al. 2002), the TCARI/OSAVI ratio can be modelled by a negative exponential curve (plots not shown). The relationship of VIs and SIF is thus also very non-linear. Nevertheless, some linearisation is possible after transformation as we will see.

The F760 has positive, somewhat linear realtionship with EVI, NDVI, NIRV vegetation indices. However, there is a big spread around a potential linear fit depending on the Cab. F687 saturates with most of the indices. The linearity is more tigh when the total SIF is considered. Total SIF vs. NDVI can be approximated with a linear model.

Total fluorescence has the potential to be used as indicator. Notably, it can be linearly modeled by NDVI. Since NDVI is a proxy for light interception, total SIF is expected to be somewhat linear to the intercepted light. Interesting is also the potential to use the slope of a linear fit between SIF total and TCARI to infer about the Chlorophyll content.

9.3 Discussion

The effect of canopy LAI and Cab is wavelength dependent. The non-linear nature of the signal is due to reabsorption effects mainly. There is a potential, however, to straighten the relationship between canopy structure and SIF by taking some transformations of the data or fit locally a linear model. TCARI and OSAVI as well as NDVI can be used to normalize the effects of canopy structure. We found a very strong linear relationship between Total SIF and NDVI, a very strong exponential decay between SIF index and Cab, and a very strong linear relationship between normalized and transformed Total SIF and Cab and LAI. In addition we defined a 4-parameter functional form for F760 as a function of Cab and LAI.
