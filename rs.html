Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 3 Remote sensing of vegetation biophysics and SIF

3.1 Remote sensing

Remote sensing (RS) is defined as the non-invasive measurement of electromagnetic radiation. Implied in this defintion is that the measurements are done from a distance (cm to km) (Gamon, in Functional Plant Ecology). Because chemical elements reflect unique spectral signature, RS can be used to infer biochemical composition (at small distances) or plant composition and diversity across larger spatial scales. The non-invasive and repetitive nature of the methods makes RS valuable approach in areas such precision agriculture, environmental monitoring and management, and carbon budgeting to name just a few.

The possibility to retrieve fluorescence from hyperspectral reflectance provides a new opportunity for the remote characterization of plant status and potentially can lead to improved estimation of global terrestrial primary productivity by transferring the Earth observation techniques from detection of canopy biophysics to the realm of ecosystem functioning (F. Magnani et al. 2014). One must consider the detectability of the process in respect to the spatiotemporal scale at which it is observed.

3.2 The concept of scale in remote sensing. Scalability of ecological processes {scale}

The term scale has many meanings in the specialized literature. In the realm of geography alone, scale refers to four distinct concepts. The first and most known concept of scale is the cartographic scale – the scale ratio of the distance between two points in the real word to the distance of these points on a map. The second interpretation of scale is the geographical scale (or extent). It describes the boundaries inside which the geographical phenomenon occurs. Often it signifies the extent of a study area or just the area of coverage of a geographical map. The third and fourth definitions of scale (operational and topographic scales, respectively) emerged together with advances of complex system research and remote sensing. These are slightly less known but it is important to be defined for each spatially distributed process.

A so called operational scale is the level of which certain geographic phenomenon operates (functions). A operational scale can only be defined over a process. It is the smallest distance at which the phenomenon is relevant and distinguishable. For example, the effect of elevation and topography on the vegetation composition have a larger operational scale in flat areas but significantly smaller operational scale in mountaneuos regions, where changes in slope and aspect can occur in several meters. The operational scale intrinsicaly depends on the definition of the process within the system. For example, in a network analysis, the daily, work-related commute from suburbs to the city has a smaller operational scale than the weekend, or holiday travel. The operational scale is not always known but very important in order to correctly choose the fourth definition of scale – the topographic scale.

The topographic scale is the level of detail depicted on a map. In the context of digital remote sensing - it is the resolution or pixel size of a remote sensing image. Especilly in the context of digital remote sensing, there is a trade of between information gain and computational burden when choosing the resolution of an image. Taking the elevation example from the previous paragraph, one should choose topographically finer scale (larger spatial resolution) to study the effects of topography in a mountaneous region, whereas a coarser topographic scale (smaller spatial resolution) can suffice in less hilly landscapes. In practice, when the exact operational scale of the proces of interest is unknown, fractal analysis can be performed to infer the best topographic scale to use.

Using this nomenclature we can define a hierarchical scaling structure of the process of fluorescence emittance (Figure 3.1).

3.3 Retrieval of SIF

3.3.1 FLD principle

The methodology used to estimate sun-induced fluorescence under natural conditions uses the fact that incoming radiation traveling through Earths atmosphere gets absorbed by elements in the atmosphere elements (the Fraunhofer line depth principle (FLD) (???). Most often, three Fraunhofer lines related to water and oxygen (telluric) absorption termed Hα, O2-B and O2-A are used for fluorescence retrieval (Figure 3.2). From these, the latter two, telluric oxygen-B and oxygen-A bands, are producing the most robust results. The main reason is that these regions also coincide relatively well with the two peaks of the fluorescence emission. The level of infilling of the fraunhofer lines depends on the amount of reflected radiation and, for vegetative surfaces, includes a contribution by sun-induced fluorescence. For example, non fluorescent surfaces will have lower recorded radiance values, while vegetation surfaces will add up to the total emission – or the level of infilling. This fact can be used to retroactively infer the amount of SIF contribution inside the wells.

knitr::include_graphics("Visuals/retrieval1.png")

Figure 3.2: Incident irradiance at the tree absorption features used for fluorescence retrieval at three different spectral resolutions. Simulated data. Source: (M. Meroni et al. 2009)

Incident irradiance at the tree absorption features used for fluorescence retrieval at three different spectral resolutions. Simulated data. Source: (M. Meroni et al. 2009)

In order to estimate the level of infilling caused by SIF, values measured outside and inside of the Fraunhofer absorption feature (inside the well and on the shoulders) are needed. Then SIF is calculated by solving the following equation system:

\[ L(\lambda_{in}) = \frac{r(\lambda_{in}) E(\lambda_{in})}{\pi} + F(\lambda_{in})\] \[ L(\lambda_{out}) = \frac{r(\lambda_{out}) E(\lambda_{out})}{\pi} + F(\lambda_{out})\]

Rewritten as:

\[r = \frac{L(\lambda_{out}) - L(\lambda_{in})}{E(\lambda_{out}) - E(\lambda_{in})} \cdot \pi\] \[F = \frac{E(\lambda_{out})L(\lambda_{in}) - L(\lambda_{out})E(\lambda_{in})}{E(\lambda_{out}) - E(\lambda_{in})}\]

where \(L_{in}\) and \(L_{out}\) are values of radiation upwelling from target outside and inside of the Fraunhofer absorption feature respectively, r is the pure reflectance (e.g. without fluorescence contribution, E is the target irradiance, and F is the fluorescence (Meroni et al., 2009). Because radiance values are being used, resulting F estimates correspond to actual amount of emitted energy in the form of electromagnetic flux in physical units (Wm-2sr-1µm-1). This calculation assumes constant F and r values across the spectrum which is not realistic. To compensate for non-constancy radiation values outside of the absorption feature are averaged across several bands in the 3FLD method (Maier et al., 2003) or additional parameterization is adopted in the cFLD method (GomezChova et al. 2006). Above mentioned methods are generally termed multispectral methods as they utilize several bands around the Fraunhofer absorption features. A specific case of cFLD is the improved FLD method (iFLD) which introduces two correction coefficients that relate the values of the fluorescence and the reflectance inside and outside of the absorption band. The novelty lies with the use of the whole spectrum around the absorption band to derive these coefficients (L. Alonso et al. 2008).

3.3.2 Spectral fitting methods – the state of the art SIF retrieval of the entire SIF spectrum

The potential of gaining additional accuracy by using the information contained across the entire spectrum is let to the development of the spectral fitting method. The rationale is to use mathematical functions to model the canopy reflectance and fluorescence at different wavelengths. In what is currently the most advanced and complex F retrieval methods, both reflectance and F are defined by spectral curve fitting, thus relaxing the previous FLD assumptions from constancy to linear/quadratic variation of both variables. By fitting cubic, spline, or higher order polynomials within the spectral windows centered at the main absorption features, discrete estimates for F inside the absorption features is calculated. However the possibility to extract the whole F spectrum by extending the fitting approach to the entire fluorescence region provides potentially further valuable information. The spectral fitting method relies on inversion of the reflectance spectrum and takes advantage of high spectral resolution configuration of new sensors in order to obtain the full spectral coverage. However, complications may occur due to the complex nature of F signal and shift of peak position due to changes in photosystem functioning. Nevertheless, this approach provides relatively high accuracy with the potential for full spectral retrieval (S. Cogliati et al. 2015).

3.4 Sensor design for SIF acquisition

Ever since it was shown SIF could be retrieved from hyperspectral data [cite], efforts have surged to design and implement measurement devices optimized for SIF retrieval. These efforts were supported through diverse scientific funding programs such as the COST actions EUROSPEC and OPTIMISE, which intended to aid the retrieval of robust SIF data, the establishment of a common measurement protocol, and the development of a global network of measurement stations. Devices suitable for measuring across wide range of spatiotemporal scales were developed and some are currently in operational stage. At leaf and canopy levels (proximal scale), retrieval of SIF is possible using high-resolution point spectrometers specifically optimized for SIF retrieval (Kebabian et al. 1999; GomezChova et al. 2006; Fournier et al. 2012; A. Burkart et al. 2015). Currently they deliver valuable information about the temporal dynamics (diurnal to seasonal) of SIF. In the air (regional scale), imaging spectrometers mounted on airplane carriers enable SIF retrieval over larger areas. UAV based lightweight spectroscopy technology will cover an intermediate (local) scale between the abovementioned proximal and regional. Finally, several efforts were focused on retrieval of SIF from satellite hyperspectral data. In the near future, a dedicated sensor on board of the eight scientific satellite mission of the European Space Agency will cover the Earth’s surface and deliver bi-weekly SIF estimates.

3.4.1 Ground sensor design

Currently, a variety of instrumental designs based on hyperspectral point-spectrometer are available. Most of them are point spectrometers. In general, ground devices include the following elements: a box housing the electronics with separate coolining compartment for spectrometers, optic fibers, installation platform (a tower or tripod), and a power supply (Figure 3.3).

knitr::include_graphics("Visuals/photospec.png")

Figure 3.3: A shematic view of PhotoSpec - a point spectrometer for SIF measurements. Source: (Grossmann et al. 2018)

Calculating reflectance requires recording incoming and reflected radiation simultaneously. This is accomplished through, either white reference panel or second upward looking optical fiber equipped with a cosine receptor capturing the hemispherical irradiance. In the former case the measuring optic fiber alternates between the target and the white reference via rotational mechanism. In the latter case, the spectrometer receives alternating signal from the downward (target) and upward (irradiance) looking optic fibers via a shutter installed between the fibers and the spectrometer.

Comparative studies showed that retrieval of fluorescence is possible with spectrometers with high SNR and high spectral resolution in the range between 650 and 800. Therefore, most designs will include a hyperspectral point-spectrometer (QE Pro Ocean optics or similar) which can be programmed at high spectral resolution in the range between 600 and 800 nm. Furthermore, in most cases a second spectrometer is also included having a wider spectral range, covering the VIS and NIR, for reference or other hyperspectral retrieval tasks.

Based on the programming of the instrument and the integration time of the spectrometer, temporal sampling of such proximal sensing devices can be as low as half a minute. This temporal resolution comes at expense of the spatial resolution. Because most ground devices are based on point spectrometer, they can only measure one point. The size of the footprint depends on the IFOV of the fiber optics and the height of installation. Nevertheless, when installed on a permanent position, such devices have helped to understand the diurnal and seasonal behavior of SIF above the canopy [cite]. It was revealed that diurnal development of SIF of unstressed crops follows the intensity of incoming light [cite], while a midday depression has been observed in stressed plants [cite].

The FLuorescence bOX (FLOX) and its precursor the SIF-system (SIFsys) were developed at the Department of Plant Sciences in Forschungszentrum Julich and currently are vital part of the ground component of SIF retrieval and validation. The FLOX instrument houses two spectrometers – FLAME and QE Pro (Ocean Optics), isolated in a cooling chamber with stable temperature. They record radiation in the visible to near infrared (VNIR) range and in the fluorescence range respectively, in both downward and upward directions through two separate fiber optics. A mechanical shutter alternates the signal between both optical fibers towards the QE Pro spectrometer (note: the alternating shutter system which was included in the original design was replaced in later designs). This almost simultaneous measurement of incoming and outgoing radiation enables calculation of spectral reflectance and subsequent SIF retrieval. Additionally, hyperspectral analysis such as calculation of narrowband hyperspectral reflectance vegetation products (VI) can be performed. Its high temporal resolution of data acquisition, waterproof design, and its flexibility in powering options, make the FLOX particularly suitable for studying seasonal dynamics of reflectance and SIF in natural conditions. Currently, data from the boxes represent the main ground measurements of SIF to be used for calibration purposes of aerial and satellite based SIF retrievals. In fact, FLOX measurements are an integral part of the FLEX sensor development. EDA of FLOX data can be seen in Chapter FLOX EDA.

3.4.2 Airborne

Airborne spectrometers can deliver high spectral and spatial resolution data on demand which improved scientific understanding of natural processes in the intermediate spatial scale. For example, increased spectral resolution helped improve our understanding of vegetation dynamics and the spatial variability of biochemical vegetation traits. The list of operational airborne spectrometer fleet is growing every year. Some of the most notable instruments in this list include APEX, AVIRIS, CASI, and HyPlant (Rascher et al. 2015). While the former three have been developed to serve as general purpose hyperspectral imagers, HyPlant was designed specifically with SIF retrieval in mind. This new airborne instrument was built with the aim to provide very high spectral resolution data in the fluorescence retrieval.

HyPlant consist of two sensing modules – a dual-channel broad band imager in the VIS and NIR spectrum (380-2500 nm) with 622 spectral bands (DUAL), and a second, high resolution imaging spectrometer (FLUO) covering the red and NIR regions (670-780 nm). The latter is specifically designed for fluorescence retrieval providing the highestto date spectral resolution in the oxygen absorption bands within the fluorescence emission spectrum (Table 1). While the design of FLUO was purposefully focused on delivering high spectral resolution in the fluorescence emission range, the second instrument (DUAL) is a more standard all-round hyperspectral imager providing data for a wide range of research questions in the traditional hyperspectral domain.In fact, the data recorded using DUAL was used to derive plant biophysical and structural parameters in chapter Retrieval. Combined with the parallel acquisition of SIF through the FLUO module it provides the unique opportunity to study the effect of canopy biophysics on the top-of-canopy SIF.(in chapter SIF-LAI relationship)

3.4.3 Spaceborne

Retrieval of SIF from space borne hyperspectral imagery have been reported from several working groups (e.g. Frankenberg et al., 2011; Joiner et al., 2012). Data from sensors such as GOSAT’s TANSO-FTS sensor, the GOME-2 sensor flying as part of the EUMETSAT Polar System MetOp mission series, and SCIAMACHY on board ENVISAT provide the needed high spectral resolution in the red to far-red range. Originally, these sensors were designed for detection and characterization of atmospheric chemistry and, thus, operate at coarse spatial resolution (≥30 km2). Newer missions , such as NASA’s OCO-2, measure at finer spatial resolution (~3 km2) while giving opportunity for SIF retrieval in the far-red (Frankenberg et al., 2014). Recently, Europe’s Sentinel-5 Precursor (TROPOMI sensor) improved the temporal scale at which sun-induced fluorescence data can be delivered (Köhler 2018). Still, the relatively large pixel size (approx. 7 km2) does not match the operational scale at which SIF becomes relevant for management practices or physiological aplications. Finer scale imagery (300 m2) in which middle to large land management units are distinguishable will be provided by the FLORIS Sensor on board of the ESA’s forthcoming eight scientific satellite mission – the Fluorescence Explorer (FLEX).

The FLEX mission is proposed as tandem concept with Sentinel-3. The coupling between the two satellites arises from the need to accurately characterize atmospheric and land surface conditions at the time of overpass. Accounting for atmospheric disturbances is important for consistent retrieval of SIF, in particular for the atmospheric correction component. At the end, data from Sentinel-3 and FLEX will be delivered as batch organized in high level data products.

The FLEX satellite will provide a global spatial coverage of the land surface between 56oS and 75oN, including coastal waters within 50 km, larger islands (>100 km2), and shallow sea waters (<10 m in depth). The equatorial crossing time is chosen to be around the highest levels of diurnal SIF yield – at 10.00. This means that the crossing over Europe will be between 10.30 for southern Spain and 11.30 for northern Finland. The revisit time is set to one month at the equator and approx. two weeks in northern latitudes. For a comprehensinve information of FLEX see the mission selection report.
