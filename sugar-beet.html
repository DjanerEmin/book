Chapter 8 Sugar beet

8.1 History, distribution and economics

Sugar beet (Beta vulgaris L.) is grown in temperate regions - latitudes between 30 and 60o N. It was cultivated some 2000 years ago and used as a garden plant alongside other beet species. The wild relative B. vulgaris ssp maritima occupies sandy, coastal, and marsh habitats.

Figure 8.1: Sugar beet plant - left flowering, right - tuber development Source: Wikipedia

The commercial value of sugar beet (B. vulgaris ssp vulgaris) arose in 18th and its importance for Europe increased after the the demise of colonial trade of sugar from sugar cane. A steady increase in dry matter yield per ha and sugar yield per ton of dry matter have been attributed to the improved husbandry, implementation of mechanization, and variety selection. Nevertheless, sugar beet provides only about 16 percent of the world’s sugar production. The rest of the demand is met by the cheaper to produce sugar from sugar cane. Europe is the largest producer of sugar beet accounting for 3/4 of worlds sugar beet production followed by North America with around 10%.

Figure 8.2: Worwide sugar beet production. Source: Wikipedia

Sugar beet is normally planted on flat areas to aid mechanisation. The crop is sown in rows 45 to 55 cm apart with sowing density of 80 to 100 000 plants per ha. The crop can be grown on wide range of pH neutral soils with medium to slightly heavy texture. Sugar beet is known to tolerate salinity for some time especially in its early development (FAO).

Water availability can have a significant effect on the final yield. Water is important during the initial development, as well as for the tuber development (Phase 3). Therefore, in areas with prolonged dry periods irrigation management is typically used to improve yields. The yield varies based on local climate conditions and implementation of husbandry (irrigation, etc). The yield for clean biomass is between 50 - 100 t clean beet / ha, while the sugar yield varies between 8 to 18 t sugar/ha.

Figure 8.3: Sugar beet fields in Germany. Left - field experiencing midday leaf wilt during the summer of 2017, Right - field with erect canopy.

US sugar beet production has seen steady decline. Instead corn based sweeteners or the cheaper sugar cane are preferred. In EU countries, sugar pricing was regulated until the end 2017 through minimum price quotas. Since the abolishment of the minimum price sugar prices are in decline, which makes the crop commercially unatractive. In addition, changing climate condition and water shortages threaten to interfere with the yield expectations.

8.2 Growth

The phenological development of sugar beet is divided in four stages (Meier1993). First and second, lasting total of 100-130 days, are the establishment of the seedling and the following crown development. The third stage is the accumulation of sugar in the root - also known as the tuber development or the ripening stage. The fourth stage is the reproductive stage, during which the flower develops. Premature flowering (bolting) can be initiated at temperature bellow 5 \(^o\)C for as little as two days. In such case the ripening stage is skipped (FAO).

8.3 Husbandry

Sugar beet grows over two year period. The reproductive stage is initiated only after a period of temperature of 5 oC and is accelarated through increas length of day. In oceanic and continental climate of Europe the crop is sown in spring and harvested in late autumn or early winter, since the commercially valuable product (the tuber) is fully grown by then. In the mediteranean region the sugar beet is sown in autumn and harvested in spring.

We will focus on the crown development (stage two) and the factors affecting the light interception, light use efficiency, and yield throught stages 2 and 3 of the phenological development.

Figure 8.4: Sugar beet phenological development. Only the commercially important first three stages are shown. Source FAO

8.4 Effects of canopy structure

8.4.1 Canopy development

The leaves of the sugar beet grow in a spiral phylotaxis forming a rossete in 5:13 spiral (e.g. there are 13 leaves for five circumverences of the spiral) (Functional Plant Ecology). Leaves are produces every 20-30 days throughout the lifetime of the plant. Due to light competition, new leaves are in general slighly smaller than the older leaf. Therefore, The total leaf area of a sugar beet reaches a maximum around mid summer and in case climate conditions are good - stays constant. In cases of environmental stress, the total leaf area might slightly decrease because production of new biomass cannot match the loss of biomass through senescence. Generally, sugar beet reaches LAI between 3 and 5 depending on soil conditions, husbandry, fertilization, and water availability. Draycott2006a

In continental Europe the sowing date is crucial for maximising yield. The crop must be sown as early as possible and quickly grow a full canopy. This maximises light interception which is generally a good predictor of final yield. However, an early sowing date might lead to bolting initiated after a prolonged cold spell in spring or even low temperature in summer. Breeders are focused on developing cold resistant varieties which can be sown earlier, and resist bolting in cases of spring and summer cold spells.

8.4.2 Effect of canopy structure on yield

A long term study in UK showed that sugar yield (t sugar per ha) is can be estimated from estimation of light interception. In their case light interception was approximated by fractional leaf cover which was derived from NDVI. On the other hand, the light interception showed a positive non linear relationship to LAI, saturating after LAI > 3.5 . It easy to see that, quicker leaf grow during the crown development and maximumisation of fractional cover are going to improve light interception and, thus, yield.

Therefore, for remote sensing applications, besides the proxies for light interception as NDVI, other proxies can be useful. Candidate predictors of yield are proxies for the absolute value of LAI and the date when this maximum is reached. The seasonal trend can be extracted.e.g. from Sentinel-2 time series at resolution good enough to infer on the individual agricultural field in Europe.

8.4.3 Effect of canopy structure on radiation budget and optical properties

Light interception is affected by many factors, we will discuss the following:

  • total leaf area,
  • leaf orientation,
  • sowing density and row spacing
  • row orientation.

Total leaf area maximises the probability of recapturing escaped photons withing canopy scattering. However, since light within the canopy generaly follows a steep extinction function, increasingly less light is captured by the extra leaf area added. This is the reason that a saturation between LAI and light interception is reached.

Changes in leaf orientation (leaf wilt) are very typical for sugar beet. They occure during warmer periods (a possible explanation of the mechanics is discussed in the water subsection). 3D modeling has shown that leaf wilting increases the percentage cover, increases the absolute amount of total sunlit leaf area, and decreases the ammount of soil visible at the sensor. We will come back to test and discuss this in chapter (LIDFab estimation and sCOPE simulation)

Feature space plot of NIR vs. red reflectance for a range of leaf wilt and Sun Zenith Angle (sz). 3D simulation

Figure 8.6: Feature space plot of NIR vs. red reflectance for a range of leaf wilt and Sun Zenith Angle (sz). 3D simulation.

Sowing density, and more so, the distance between rows determines the amount of exposed soil. Exposed soil lowers overal light use efficiency per square unit. It also has higher evaporation potential and can lead to water loss. Sometimes, thinning is implemented at the end of plant initiation (4-8 leaves) to leave 3 - 6 plants per meter row. Rows are typically 40-50 cm apart which enables the crop to reach maximum LAI while fully closing the canopy.

Row azimuth orientation is important for satellite images with coarser spatial resolution. The row orientation relative to sun-view angle has an effect on the total radiation through changing the proportion of sunlit soil viewed by the sensor. Temporal simulations of a 3D sugar beet canopy have shown that, especially at higher LAD angles, total radiation can change up to 20% depending on row azimuth angle. The main driver of change in total radiation is the changing proportion of sunlit and shadowed soil with solar Zenith angle. The proportion of sunlit vegetation is less sensitive to solar azimuth, especially at planophyll canopies.

8.5 Other effects

8.5.1 effect of nitrogen

Nitrogen fertilization improves yield through providing resource for the production of biomass. Nitrogen increases the maximum possible LAI reached as well as the speed of growth during the shoot development stage (Phase 2) .Typically, maximum LAI is reached in July and it is maintained under good soils. Under low nitrogen conditions the LAI decreases in favor of carbohydrate storage in the tuber.

8.5.2 Effect of soil water

Sugar beet reacts to lower soil water potential by closing stomata to restrict water loss (so called anisohydric species). The decrease in leaf water content has a negative effect on leaf hydrostatic pressure (turgor) which maintains the leaf structure and is important for growth. This is opposite to isohydric species which maintain their leaf water potential even at lower soil water potential (such as corn).

Sugar beet has stomata on both sides of the leaf (amphistomatic) with more on the lower (abaxial) side. In field conditions leaf water potential is sensitive to air to leaf vapour pressure deficit (VPD) and decreases even when soil water is enough. One possible explanation is that at high evaporative demand from the air, the hydralylic resinstance of water conductive pathways limits the flux of water to leaves leading to leaf wilting. To prevent further evaporative water loss, stomata close which leads to lowering of photosynthetic efficiency (Some data suggests that xylem size and structure is responsible for the slow water conductance).

Young sugar beet leaves maintain higher stomatal conductance than older leaves, they also wilt at lower leaf water potential (observed on the field, and simulated LIDFab show the same results) (Draycott 2006).
