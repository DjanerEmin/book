Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 15 Exploring the relationship between LAI and Chlorophyll and SIF

In chapter 9 we explored the effect of LAI and Cab on SIF using simulated data. In chapter 11 we saw that the relationship is visible at ground level observations. In this chapter we will build increasingly complex models which are seeking to explain the spatial variability in SIF as a function of LAI, Cab, and spatial location in the area.

15.1 Data and RQ

15.1.1 RQ

RQ1. Which are the significant predictors of SIF in O2A band inside of intensively managed agro-environment?

RQ2. Does the spatial location have a significant effect on the interaction between SIF signal and LAI? RQ3. What proportion of variation of SIF can be explained by canopy biophysics and/or spatial location.

RQ4. What is the appropriate form a of the model to be used to predict SIF?

RQ5. How does spatial aggregation (scaling up to coarser resolution) affects the above explanatory power and the shape of the relationship(within field and between field variability)?

To answer research questiona 1 through 3 we classify the predictors into two groups - those related to plant structure (internal), and those related to the environmental conditions (external) and evaluate the significance of the predictors using a stepwise regression modelling. The internal factors are the leaf arrea index (LAI) and chlorophyll content (Cab) retrieved in chapter 14. The external factors include the position in NS and WE directions, the soil conditions and wether the fields are located in the upper or lower terrace.

According to our previous observations in chapter 9 the appropriate model shape of the relationship between SIF and LAI should be a postive with saturation (Figure 9.4). In addition, the absense of green biomass indicates the absense of fluorescence emittance, hence, the appropriate model form should have a zero-intercept plateauing at high LAI values.

To answer research quesiton 4 we will fit a linear model with 0-intercept (base line for comparison), a linear model with plateau, a piecewise linear models and the more flexible generalized additive model (GAM) to a training subset and evaluate it against an independent test subset by investigate the model diagnostics after (???). We will discuss the evaluation statistics, the signficance of predictors, and the considerations for choosing the best model.

To answer the last research questions we will consider two spatial scales. First, we will use the nominal resolution of HyPlant sensor (1m) within the boudaries of three agricultural fields (within field variability). Secondly, we will aggregate SIF and the other relevant parameters for each agro-field/soil type combination and use these average values as the nominal sampling units for the whole extend of the Selhausen study area.

15.1.2 Data

The data set used here is the same as in the previous two chapters. The fluorescence (iFLD) was acquired the HyPlant sensor (Section 3.4.2) over the Selhausen study site (Chapter 14.1.2) on 26.06.2016. The nominal spatial resolution of the image is 1 meter. The area covered by the scene is approximately 27 km2 and includes 58 sugar beet fields. Sugar beet was choosen because it was the most dominant green crop at time of image acquisition. For descriptive statistics of SIF in sugar beet see chapter 13.

Leaf area index and Chlorophyll content were retrieved for the same image by means of RT model inversion (ProSAIL) (Chapter 14).

The soil type, depth, and bedrock information were extracted from a soil map from the german goverment (Section 7.4). The terrace boundaries were defined after (Rudolph et al. 2015). Elevation was extracted from a stereo-camera system on board of HyPlant.

Standardization of all input variables was performed prior to model fit.

15.2 Field level

To study the within field variability we will concentrate on the group of three fields in the center of the selhausen image (Figure 15.1). Since they are adjecent to each other they can be regarded as one single field. Henceforth, the analysis performed with data from the three fields will be called field scale analysis to indicate that the analysis extends to a single field (e.g. a single management unit). Analysis on this level explores to what degree the interaction between SIF and LAI can be modelled within single agricultural field.

15.2.1 Data partitioning

The field level data set consist of 84474 pixels. The image was sampled equally (n=200) from each of the eight soil types (Figure 15.1). A stratification based on soil substrate was chosen because some of the soil types are underrepresented in the data. The resulted random sample with equal proportion of pixels from each soil class was further split into train and test subsets (70/30). It is quite obvious that there is a significiant noise in the system (Figure 15.2).

15.2.2 Models on the field level

15.2.2.1 Univariate linear model with 0-intercept against LAI

We discussed in the introduction that an appropriate form for a linear fit is one with 0-intercept term with plateau, e.g. saturating at high LAI.

This model is often used to model responses which saturate after some break point value. The fit after the max value is a horizontal line. In essense it is a linear model for the range between 0 and the saturating point for LAI. After this saturating LAI any SIF equals SIFmax. A linear model with no plateau will be used as a reference model for comparison and evaluation.

Figure 15.3: Fit on the training data for the linear and Piecewise linear models (linear response with plateau). In both cases a 0-intercept model was fit

For both models, the assumption of independence of residuals is violated the edges of the distribution (plots not shown). The plots above answer our expectations of poor performance. Most of the variation remains unexplained. In addition, violation of assumptions and existence of observations with high levarage suggest the single factor (LAI) is unable to explain tha variability between SIF and LAI at the nominal 1m resolution. The predictive performance of both models were evaluated using the diagnostics in Gouch (2003). These are presented in the table 15.1.

The interpretation of the error decomposition after Gauch is given in section 17.1.6.1.

The overall error MSD improves from simple linear to a linear with plateau. The error decomposition showed that NU LC combined explain most of the prediction error. For the linear regression with a plateau the rotation and bias decreased while the LC increased. Though the linear model with plateau is overal better it underperforms in tracking the noise, e.g. the all values after LAImax are modeled via one single value - SIFmax.

To minimize the slope and exlain more of the variation, more complex local regressions can be used. For example, splines fit a linear model to a weighted regions track better curvy relationships. On the other hand, the noise in the system indicates additional variability to be explained. We will seek to explain this variation and investigate its directions by further including Cab, soil as explanatory covariates. In conclusion, the SIF LAI relationship is visible at one meter resolution but is quite noisy, the apporpriate model form is one with saturation at high values but flexible enough to track complex changes.

15.2.2.2 Multivariate. Including Cab and Soil

In the previous section we performed bivariate modeling, seeking to measure the proportion of variation in SIF explained by LAI. Here, we will include chlorophyl concentration and spatial information (soil type) to the linear response model with plateau, and to a generalized additive spline model (GAM).

By plotting the individual additive components of a spline GAM, we can interogate the effect of each of the three input variables (Figure ??). In figure 15.5 \(x1 \equiv LAI\), \(x2 \equiv Cab\), and \(x3 \equiv\) the soil type. However, we cannot conclude about the significance of each of the predictors.

In the SCOPE simulation (Chapter 9) we saw that SIF760 saturates at high LAI and Cab. At the hyplant level we observa that SIF and LAI have positive, fairly linear relationship while the SIF decreases at high Cab. One possible explanation is that Cab actually contains the combined information from high LAI and high Cab.

15.2.3 Summary

We saw that ordinary least squares (OLS) is not appropriate to model the highly complex SIF ~ LAI relationship. OLS relies on certain assumptions about the data and the appropriate sample size. For a large sample size will artificially inflate the regression dignastic statistics. The optimal sample size for OLS is between 30 and several hundred. Also, if the magnitutes of the input variables are different, standardization is necessary. The SIF and LAI ranges fairly similar but not comparable to Cab and elevation. VI have different ranges too. Simple ratios are bound to infinity, normalized indices - bound to 0 and 1.

Signal to noise ratio is low at this scale. The largest part of the overal error (MSD) is due to residual noise even for models with visually significant rotation between predicted and observed from validation. For the leanr models with plateau, including Cab does not improve overal error, suggesting that the added noise by Cab is indeed higher than the added information. The best model, smooth splines, is only slightly better than chance.

improvements - better validation of LAI and Cab, including better parameterization (e.g. Leaf Angle). Non-parameteric retrieval methods ( ML). Better SIF retrieval. will reduce input noise. Further non-parametric models to improve relationship. although some oare too black box so there is a trade of between interpreation nad prediction.

15.3.2 Stewise model selection

Here we will use the leaps exhaustive stepwise procedure to fit full interaction model between LAI, Cab, soil, and terrace association. The procedure will build increasingly complex models (including more explanatory variables) and evaluate the goodness-of-fit against parsimony. That is the information gain vs. added complexity. The best model is found by minimizing the Mallows’Cp estimate. In figure ?? the Cp estimate is plotted against the number of explanatory variables. In figure ?? the important variables are shown.

## Reordering variables and trying again:

The most parsimonious model contains the following predictors:

## [1] "(Intercept)"                  "LAI"                          "soilnClay"                    "LAI:Cab"                     
## [5] "LAI:soilnPseudoclay"          "LAI:in.high2TRUE"             "soilnPseudoclay:in.high2TRUE"

Cab appears only as an interaction term. The Clay and Pseudoclay are the two most dominant soil types of the lower terrace. Both of them have an effect on SIF760 - clay has direct effect (factorial) pseudoclay through interaction with LAI. In addition, the pseudoclay in the higher terrace have positive effect- these are good soils. The interaction between LAI and terrace which was visible by the plot above (B) is captured too.

The LAI and the conditions in the field are the two most important predictors for SIF. At the agrienvironmental scale -Cab is less important and appears only as an interaction term with LAI.

15.4 Discussion

Parametric regression is advantageous when seeking to understand a process because if the assumptions for normallity and indipendence are true, it can yield robust inquiries and models. These models can be used and be used to predict.

Two paramaters related to canopy structure (boiphysics) were considered in this chapter - LAI and Cab. These two parameter have a positive saturating overal effect on SIF. the reason for positive relationship is obvious - more green vegetation per square area leads to area from whereSIF is emitted. However, this also mean increased internal scattering by the structure and higher chance for reabsorption by chlorophyll. Similarly, more chlorophyl molecules per gram tissue increases the chance of reabsorption thus leading to saturation at high values. The shapes for the saturating curve of SIF vs. LAI and Cab, can be observed in controlled experiment (e.g solution of chlorophyll or at the leaf area manipulation experiments) or they can be retrieved via RT simulation(Fluorsail).

At the remote sensing side, decoupling the effect of biophysical characteristics on SIF using maps with medium to high spatial resolution can be used to improve crop modeling. In addtion, improved physiological inference inside the field can also be usefull in early stress warning, crop prediction and other agricultural applications etc. At the larger scale, it can improve photosynthetic models and thus estimates of global carbon uptake.
There are several papers showing relationship between SIF and canopy biophyisical parameters or proxies thereof (mostly VIs) at different scales. For example relating SIf to vegetation indice such as NDVI, NIRv, peiqi. at small scale, airborne and satellite levels, as well as many simulation outputs have shown similar results.
