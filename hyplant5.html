Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 17 Evaluation of proxy estimates for canopy structure for spatial upscaling of Top-of-Canopy Sun-induced Fluorescence

The possibility to extract chlorophyll fluorescence from vegetation reflectance enables scaling up the physiological and fluorescence research from the individual plant to the ecosystem (G. H. Mohammed et al. 2019). The top of the canopy (TOC) steady state sun-induced fluorescence (SIF) can be retrieved from variety of measurement systems across a wide range of spatiotemporal scales (Aasen et al. 2019). For example, TOC SIF was succesfully retrieved from tower (A. Burkart et al. 2015; Grossmann et al. 2018), airborne (Rascher et al. 2009; Rascher et al. 2015; Cook et al. 2013), and satellite based spectral data (Coppo et al. 2017; Köhler et al. 2018). An obvious tradeoff exists between the spatial extend and the temporal coverage among those three groups of sensors. Tower based systems provide insights on the temporal behaviour of SIF in response to diurnal and seasonal variations of environmental conditions but are constrained in footprint size (Joiner et al. 2014). Satellite retrieval of SIF enables large scale ecosystem analysis (Guanter et al. 2014) but at comparably coarser temporal frequency, while the intermediate airborne sensors are capable of delivering SIF data which shares some of the advantages of the former two groups but have higher operational costs.

In the future, global spatiotemporal coverage of SIF will be significantly improved through the launch of ESA’s FLEX satellite mission (Drusch et al. 2016). The FLEX satellite will deliver data at 300 m nominal pixel resolution with a bi-mothly revisit period. The spatial resolution of FLEX still presents challenges related to linking tower based obseration of SIF and GPP to the satellite grid cell especially in highly heterogeneous habitats. A possible solution is the incorporation of an intermediate scaling procedure using spectral data with finer spatial but insufficient for SIF retrieval spectral resolution (e.g. Sentinel-2, airborne or drone). By providing extendend spatial and at least some diurnal coverage through repeated acquisitions, data at this scale is suitable for physiological inference at the field level and have the potential to see applications in precision agriculture or as intermediate scalers between the tower and satellite sensors.

In order to use such intermediate data to indirectly infer about the variability of SIF the main drivers of TOC SIF must be known. Among those, the effect of scattering and reabsorption of SIF within the canopy (He et al. 2017; M. Meroni et al. 2009; S. Cogliati et al. 2015) via the canopy structure is particularly important because it varies among ecosystems, species, spatial location and time of the day. Indeed, experimental and modeling studies consistently attribute uncertainty or variability of SIF to the effect of canopy structure. For example, in an elaborate field set up SIF variability was linked to the location of the leaf inside the canopy (F. Pinto et al. 2016). In (R. Colombo, Meroni, and Rossini 2016) it was shown that fluorescence and apparent fluorescence yield are covarying with leaf area index (LAI) but when divided by simple ratio vegetation index (VI) the relationship to LAI was lost. Also, sensitivity analysis of SCOPE model (C. van der Tol et al. 2009) showed that the amounts of TOC SIF is mainly governed by parameters related to canopy structure (J. Verrelst et al. 2015). Using FluorSAIL (Rhoul et al. 2014) demonstrated that the diurnal variation of TOC SIF depends on LAI and leaf inclination, with higher LAI values generally diminishing the SIF diurnal variation (saturation effects). So, it is obvious that any spatial downscaling must include a proxy estimate for the effect of canopy structure on the TOC SIF.

There is another important reason to search for the best proxy for canopy structure. Because the valuable information about photosynthetic efficiency is contained in the quantum yield of fluorescence (emited fluorescence per quant absorbed PAR) (Genty, Briantais, and Baker 1989; Porcar-Castell et al. 2014) scaling back the TOC SIF to that emitted by a single photosystem is needed. Hence, the estimation of the quantum yield of fluorescence, which is easily retrievable via PAM-fluorimetry (Maxwell and Johnson 2000), can only be done after taking the effects of the canopy structure into account and requires an accurate account of the modulation effect of canopy structure.

A formal definition of theoretical scaling factor for the effect of scattering and reabsorption can be defined following the Monteith light use efficiency paradigm. Chlorophyll fluorescence, being an energy quant, obeys by the rules of energy conservation, thus, it can be expressed at any measurement scale as:

\[\begin{equation} SIF = PAR \cdot \Gamma \cdot \phi F \label{eq:monteith} \end{equation}\]

where \(PAR\) is the incoming shortwave radiation in the photosynthetically active region of the solar spectrum (400 - 800 nm), \(\phi F\) is the fluorescence quantum yield, and \(\Gamma\) is a theoretical scalar asigning the proportion of attenuation of SIF to the relevant factors at that scale, i.e. the effect of geometry (Yang 2018). At canopy scale \(\Gamma\) is defined as \(\Gamma = fPAR\cdot\sigma_{CL}\), where \(fPAR\) is the fraction of absorbed PAR, and \(\sigma\) is a SIF scattering coefficient. Similar formulations were defined for PSII level in (Gu et al. 2019; Porcar-Castell et al. 2014).

At any scale, the theoretical scalar (\(\Gamma\)) cannot be measured directly opening the need for finding appropriate proxy measurements. At canopy scale, relevant for remote sensing, top-down spectrally variant and invariant analytical models defined the theoretical scalar as a function of canopy interceptance and albedo (Yang and van der Tol 2018; X. Liu et al. 2019; Romero, Cordon, and Lagorio 2018; Ramos and Lagorio 2004; Badgley, Field, and Berry 2017). Typically, these are tested and validated using bottom-up simulations. It remains to be seen the degree to which this scalar can be found from measurement data alone and wether and how equation () in combination with a proxy for \(\Gamma\) can be used to breach the gap between measurements of SIF done at different scales. While a scaling procedure is easy to be outlined given equation (), the complexity is contained in finding the best proxy for the scalar.

In this paper we defined a scaling procedure based on equation () usable to relate remote sensing and physiological data collected at different operational scales and apply the scaling approach to test the performance of four proxies for \(\Gamma\). Spatially distributed SIF was predicted for one agricultural field based on the spatial distribution of the candidate canopy structure proxies and one reference location where SIF is known. The resulting model SIF image was evaluated against the observed SIF for the field by decomposing the error into three easily interpretable statistics. The sensitivity of the method against variation of the reference location was evaluated using a Monte Carlo analysis. The valuable information contained in the spatial distribution of the residuals was investigated and possibility to use this approach not only for scaling but also for physiological inference was discussed.

17.1 Methods

17.1.1 Theoretical background

At the canopy scale, \(\Gamma\) is the multiplicative of \(fPAR \cdot \sigma F\), where \(\sigma F\) is a coefficient accounting for scattering and both \(\sigma F\) and \(fPAR\) are spectrally dependent (X. Liu et al. 2019). The scattering coefficient was analytically derived as the ratio between reflectance at specific wavelength (\(R_{(\lambda)}\)) and canopy interceptance (\(i_0\)) and leaf albedo \(\omega_{L(\lambda)}\) (Yang 2018; X. Liu et al. 2019), while \(fPAR\) can be modeled as a function of light absorbing pigments \(f_{(C_{ab})}\) (X. Liu et al. 2019). Thus, a definition of the effect of scattering and reabsorption, separating the effects of chlorophyll content and canopy structure is:

\[\begin{equation} \Gamma_{(\lambda)} = f_{(C_{ab})} \cdot \frac{R_{(\lambda)}}{i_0. \omega_{L(\lambda)}} \label{eq:gamma} \end{equation}\]

17.1.2 Rescaling SIF. Location model for high resolution remote sensing data (HyPlant)

We can extend the formulation of equation () into the spatiotemporal domain by indexing for any location \(l\) and any time \(t\):

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{l(t)} = \frac{1}{\pi}PAR_{l(t)}.\Gamma_{l(t)}.\phi F_{l(t)} \label{eq:montspt} \end{equation}\]

where \(l \in [1:n]\) is a location (pixel) on a remote sensing image with size \(n\) pixels, and \(t\) - time of image acquisition. In this paper we will consider only a location model by keeping \(t = const\) and varying only \(l\). However, the concept can be extended to include variable time. An extension of the model, a spatiotemporal model, is defined and a test case outlined in the supplementary material for this article.

Considering a single image acquisition by a low flying imaging spectrometer such as in (Rascher et al. 2015) lets us asume a constant time (\(t = const\)) across the entire image and constant spatial distribution of incoming radiation (\(PAR_l = const\) for all \(l\)). Taking those two considerations into account, equation () can be rewritten for the spatial domain by dropping the time index \(t\) for all inputs and the location index \(l\) for \(PAR\):

\[\begin{equation} SIF_{l} = \frac{1}{\pi}PAR .\Gamma_{l}.\phi F_{l} \label{eq:locationmodel} \end{equation}\]

The equation above shows that the spatial variability of SIF recorded for a small area is governed by the location specific canopy structure (\(\Gamma\)) and physiology (\(\phi F\)), but is independent from \(PAR\).

For any reference locations \(r_{1\dotso m}\subset l\) from the image ( \(r\) is a subset of \(l\), \(m\) is the number of reference locations, where \(m \leq n\) ) we solve the location model for both the reference location and for all pixels :

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:locationmodel2} SIF_{l_{1 \dotso n}} = \frac{1}{\pi}PAR.\Gamma_{l_{1\dotso n}}.\phi F_{l_{1\dotso n}} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:locationmodel3} SIF_{r} = \frac{1}{\pi}PAR.\Gamma_{r}.\phi F_{r} \end{equation}\]

By solving equations () and () for \(PAR\) and combining the two we have:

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:locationmodel4} SIF_{l_{1 \dotso n}} = SIF_{r}\frac{\Gamma_{l_{1\dotso n}}.\phi F_{l_{1\dotso n}}}{\Gamma_{r}.\phi F_{r}} \end{equation}\]

Equation () links TOC SIF for any two arbitrary locations \(l\) and \(r\) inside a single remote sensing image (Figure 1 left). The second term on the right side is a scaling factor describing the relative difference in TOC SIF between the two locations to be governed by structural and physiological factors. In other words, if canopy structure and physiological status for any two locations \(r\) and \(l\) are the same, then TOC SIF for both will be equal plus a random white noise, e.g. \(SIF_l = SIF_r. 1 + \xi\)

We can further simplify equation () by assuming that locations \(l\) and \(r\) exhibit similar physiological status, e.g. \(\phi F_{l_{1\dotso n}} \approx \phi F_{r}\) so that the ratio \(\frac{\phi F_{l_{1\dotso n}}}{\phi F_{r}} \approx 1\). By doing this, we assign all spatial variability of TOC SIF solely to the effects of canopy structure. This is a very serious assumption and we will come back to this in the discussion. Then, a final simplified form of a location model comparing any two locations within a single remote sensing image is:

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:locationmodel6} SIF_{l_{1 \dotso n}} = \frac{SIF_{r}}{\Gamma_{r}}.\Gamma_{l_{1\dotso n}} + \xi \end{equation}\]

The \(\frac{SIF_{r}}{\Gamma_{r}}\) term in equation () we call the reference scalar. The reference scalar comes solely from the reference location inside the map. As such, equation () constitutes a link between measurements of SIF done at two different spatial scales. The reference scalar ranges between \((0:\infty)\) and is not defined for reference locations with no vegetation (e.g. when \(\Gamma_r \leq 0\)).

In this paper we will use equation () to predict the SIF in one agricultural field using information about the location canopy structure \(\Gamma_l\) from an airborne image. The complexity is to find an appropriate proxy for the effect of canopy structure, thus we will evaluate the performance of several candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\).

Raster processing scheme

17.1.3 Data used

17.1.3.1 Site

The study site is an agricultural field (50o51’46.9“N 6o27’07.9”E) sown with sugar beet (Beta vulgaris L.) situated in the TERENO research site Selhausen. The field is situated on the East bank of the Rur river on sits over fluvial deposits from the Holocene and a Pleistocene sand and gravel sediments (Rudolph et al. 2015). A weak slope exist in W-E direction (Figure ). The typical climate is oceanic with mean annual air temperature around 10 \(^oC\), and mean annual precipitation around 700mm.

17.1.3.2 Remote sensing image

The area was mapped with a pushbroom imaging spectrometer system (HyPlant) flying at altitude 680 meters. The HyPlant instrument consists of a narrowband spectrometer with 623 spectral channels in the range 350-2500 nm (VNIR), and high spectrally resolved module designed for retrieval of sun-induced fluorescence (FLUO) (Rascher et al. 2015). The image acuquisition was performed on 26th June 2018 under clear sky conditions (DOY:177, LT:15:30) at 680 m above the ground (sampling resolution 1m).

The data was first atmospherically corrected in raw geometry and the resulting top-of-canopy reflectance was georectified in a later processing step [Siegmann in this issue]. The candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\) (see section below) were calculated using the reflectance data from the VNIR sensor, while TOC SIF (\(SIF_l\)) was retreived using iFLD from FLUO [Damm]. A 3x3 running average filter was applied on the fluorescence data. The image was cropped to the exact borders of the agricultural field (Figure ).

17.1.3.3 Ground measurements

At time of observation the sugar beet plant had a fully developed canopy (12 to 15 leaves) and covered more betwee 80% and 95% of the ground (BBCH = 39). The distance between rows was 45 cm with approximately 8.4 plants occupying one square meter. A visual difference in plant size and viguor was observed between the West and the East portions of the field along the elevation gradient. Subsurface soil moisture declined along the elevation gradient from 8% in the West to 3% in the East section of the field (data not shown, the field was subject of intensive collection of auxiliary data for further insights readers are referred to the PhD work of the first author, in prep).

Spectroscopic and auxilary data was gathered at 15 locations inside the field and used to validate the remote sensing images used in this study or to estimate parameters used in the calculation of the canopy structure proxy. The locations were sampled along two transects in the time span +/- 2 hours around solar noon on 26th and 27th June (DOY: 177, DOY: 178). The transects were drawn in the direction of highest elevation gradient (Figure bottom left) which also represents the highest variability of plant LAI.

17.1.3.4 Spectroscopic data

The spectroscopic data was collected using a FLOX device carried on a backpack (JB Hyperspectral). FLOX is an automated field spectroscopy device designed to collect unattended, continuous, long-term hyperspectral measurements.
The optical fibers were attached to a leveling device to ensure orthogonal view at all time. At each location, spectroscopic measurements of incoming and reflected radiation were performed from a height ~ 220 cm (IFOV = 25o). Measurements lasted for between 5 and 15 minutes at each location.

Top-of-canopy SIF was retrielved from the spectroscopic data using the iFLD method [@] via a stand alone processing script distributed by the manufacturer (JB Hyperspectral). The core functions of the data processing are contained in two R packages (FieldSpectroscopyCC and FieldSpectroscopyDP) openly available on GitHub under the GNU v3.0 licence. This data was used to validate the TOC SIF retrieved by the airborne sensor (Supplementary materials).

17.1.3.5 Canopy structure and leaf albedo

LAI was estimated non-destructively using SunScan photometer (Delta-T Devices). At each location the “wand” was inserted under the canopy in three orthogonal directions and 5 readings of transmitted radiance were measured. The leaf angel distribution parameter (LADP) was kept constant at 1.8 - a typical value for sugar beet. This data was used to validate the LAI estimate retrieved from the airborne sensor (Supplementary materials).

Leaf optical properties were measured using ASD and FluoWat leaf clip in 15 randomly selected leaves inside the field. Reflectance and transmittance were used to estimate the leaf albedo (Supplement S3).

For the estimation of light extinction inside the canopy two days of diurnal measurements of incoming light above the canopy (\(I_0\)) and transmitted light below the canopy (\(I\)) were taken using SunScan photometer (Delta-T Devices). The measurements were performed every 15 minutes between 9:00 and 18:00 LT (Supplementary materials).

17.1.4 Selection of candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\)

The canopy and leaf level parameters collected during the field work were used to calculate spectrally variant analytical \(\Gamma\) after (X. Liu et al. 2019). In addition, three vegetation indices (VI) already tested as proxies for canopy structure were used.

17.1.4.1 Analytical \(\Gamma\)

In (X. Liu et al. 2019) the functional form for the effect of canopy structure on the probability of fluorescence escaping was found by training a random forest machine on simulated data. Here, for the absorptance term we will use a proxy for \(fPAR_{chl} = k_2.WDRVI\) where \(WDRVI = (\alpha R_{nir} - R_{red})/(\alpha R_{nir} + R_{red})\) (Vina and Gitelson 2005) (X. Liu et al. 2019). The \(\alpha\) parameter typically ranges between 0.1 and 0.2 (Vina and Gitelson 2005) while the scaling coefficient \(k_2\) ranges between 0.78 and 0.80 for leaf chlorophyll content from 20 to 60 \(\mu g/cm^2\). A non-linear least squares (NLS) routine on a separate training set yielded best \(\alpha = 0.15\) and \(k_1 = 0.65\) since the chlorophyll content retrieved from leaf reflectance ranged between 40 and 60 \(\mu g/cm^2\) inside the field (data not shown).

The interceptance (\(i_0\)) was modelled using an exponential extinction function \(i_0 = 1-exp(-k_1LAI)\) as in (Yang and Tol 2018), where the extinction coefficient\(k_2\) coefficient was calculated as the mean from the two day diurnal measurements of incoming radiation above (\(I_o\)) and bellow (\(I_i\)) the canopy applying \(k_1 = - ln(I_i/I_o)cos(\theta)/LAI\) (equation (3) in (L. Zhang et al. 2014)). In the same study the extinction coeffiecient is estimated between 0.4 and 0.6 for sugar beet, however, our measurements were performed in the densest part of the field and therefore biased towards denser canopies yielding an estimate of \(k_1 = 0.7\).

LAI was estimated from the soil-adjusted vegetation index \(SAVI = \frac{(R_{850 - R_{650}) \cdot 1.5}}{(R_{850} + R_{650} + 0.5)}\) using standardized parameterization in ATCOR processing software as \(LAI = -\frac{1}{0.65} ln(\frac{0.72 - SAVI}{0.61})\). The retrieved LAI showed a moderate agreement with the LAI measured on the ground. The estimates for LAI divereged at high values, however, this will have little effect on the final calculation of \(\Gamma\) since the sensitivity of LAI on the calculation drops exponentially with increase in LAI.

Finally, leaf albedo (\(\omega\)) was found from the multiangular leaf meausements performed on 14 leaves as the sum of leaf reflectance and transmittance (\(\omega_{(\lambda)} = \rho_{(\lambda)} + \tau_{(\lambda)}\)).

17.1.4.2 Vegetation indices

In addition to the analytically derived \(\Gamma\), we compiled a list of three computationaly inexpensive vegetation indices suggested as candidate proxies for canopy structure. At least for satellite observations these indices have shown promising results (Badgley, Field, and Berry 2017; ???; Yang 2018).

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:NIRv} NIRv = NDVI . R_{nir} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:FCVI} FCVI = R_{nir} - R_{vis} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:NDVI} NDVI = \frac{R_{nir} - R_{vis}}{R_{nir} + R_{vis}} \end{equation}\]

17.1.5 Monte Carlo approach to find the best reference location

We sampled \(m=\) 400 reference locations (\(r\)) equally from three regions on the map (lower, middle and upper parts of the study field) to cover the covariation of \(\Gamma_r\) and \(SIF_r\) (Figure top right). We predicted a raster (\(SIF_l\)) based on equation () for each of the four proxies for \(\Gamma\). In effect, we perform a type of Monte Carlo analysis by repeatedly picking a single random reference location and calcualating a theoretical SIF based on equation (). The predicted SIF image was evaluated against true observed SIF measured by HyPlant (Figure 1 right). The valuable information lies in the residual variation between the modelled SIF and true observed SIF not explained by the rescaling method.

17.1.6 Evaluation statistics

17.1.6.1 Error decomposition and sensitivity to the reference scalar

The predictive performance of the four candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\) used to calculate SIF based on equation () was evaluated using the coeficient of determination (\(R^2\)) and the mean squared deviation (MSD) between modeled and observed SIF. Furthermore, the MSD was partitioned into squared bias (SB), non-unity slope (NU), and lack of correlation (LC)(Gauch, Hwang, and Fick 2003). The MSD and its decomposition are defined as:

\[\begin{equation} MSD = \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y_i})^2 \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} SB = \big(\overline{\hat{y}} - \overline{y}\big)^2 \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} NU = (1 - b^2) \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \big( \hat{y_i} - \overline{\hat{y}} \big)^2 \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} LC = (1 - r^2) \frac{1}{n} \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \big( \hat{y_i} - \overline{y} \big)^2 \end{equation}\]

where \(b\) is the slope of the least squares regression of actual values on the predicted values, and \(r^2\) is the square of the correlation coefficient between actual and predicted.

The four statistics above have the relation: \(MSD = SB + NU + LC\), meaning that the total evaluation error consist of bias, gain, and lack of correlation. interpretable respectively as:

  1. systemic over- or under-estimation (translation),
  2. non-linear relation between observation and prediction (rotation),
  3. and spread around the 1:1 line (scatter).

Significant translation or rotation indicates that the model form is incorrect, while a significant scatter indicates that the model does no describe the system sufficiently well - there is a missing explenatory variables, or the system has significant noise.

The sensitivity of the the proposed rescaling model to the selection of reference location was examined by plotting the MSD, NU, and SB against the input reference \(\Gamma_r\) and \(SIF_r\).

17.1.6.2 Spatial pattern of residuals

The residual unexplained variation for the best model SIF for each proxy was mapped and a spatial pattern analysis was performed on the residual maps to check wether there is significant spatial autocorrelation or clustering of the residuals. We examined visually the degree of clustering, the replication of existing, or emergence of new spatial patterns. The degree of spatial autocorrelation was quantified using local and global Moran’s I (Anselin 1995). The Global I ranges between -1 and 1, with 0 for perfectly random spatial distribution of values.

17.2 Results

17.2.1 Interpretation of spatial patterns of true SIF, VIs, and calculated analytical \(\Gamma\)

The HyPlant image acquisition was done on the first clear sky day following a prolonged overcast period. The sugar beet was fully developed however, visible differences in the amount of green foliage and leaf architecture existed between the higher and lower elevations - higher and lower terraces. Based on that, the field can be divided into three regions, lower flat elevation with mid to high canopy density, vigorous mid section along the slope, and a patchy, low canopy coverage region in the higher terrace (Figure ). Leaf wilting with varying severity was observed in the afternoon hours at time of image acquisition. The wilting developed with different pace depending on the location with plants in lower terrace resisting wilt for longer time. Since the image was taken after a period of overcast weather with sporadic rain events, no sustainable water stress is observed. However, given the diurnal wilt of leaves, possible short term stomatal response has likely occurred in some parts in the field.

The spatial distributioni of red SIF (\(SIF_{687}\)) follows a smooth increasing trend from West to East along the elevation gradient (Figure A). Similar pattern was observed and discussed in (X. Liu et al. 2019) for an adjacent agricultural field. The authors suggested bidirectional effects (BRDF) related to sensor viewing angle as the cause arguing that the field is otherwise homogeneous. While BRDF plays an important role in SIF retrieval (HyPlant has -16.7 to -16.7 view zenith angle across track), we cannot exclude the possibility that the trend is linked to internal factors rather than external sensor bias. Contrary to the statement in (X. Liu et al. 2019), the field conditions in the surroundings are highly variable as a result of the paleo-geological history of the area (Rudolph et al. 2015). Given the fast drainage the soil moisture might be linked to the observed SIF pattern but providing a mechanistic link is beyond the scope of this work.

Beside the W-E trend on the \(SIF_{687}\) map, only one canopy related feature is clearly visible on the \(SIF_{687}\) map - a patch with severely reduced canopy cover in the higher terrace is picked up as reduction in \(SIF_{687}\). On the contrary, the spatial pattern of \(SIF_{760}\) (Figure B) closely resembles that of the four candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\) and no W-E trend is visible as in \(SIF_{687}\). The highest amount of SIF in the far red is emitted in the mid vigorous part, while the lower terrace is widely homogeneous. Larger patches with lower vegetation cover are seen in the edges and in the higher terrace. The cutline trailing along the 109.5 elevation isoline was also clearly observable in the field. The observed patterns in \(SIF_{760}\) are not surprising since scattering \(\sigma\) in the far red is influenced primarily by structure. Hence, a proper derivation of the geometric factor \(\Gamma\) will benefit physiological inference at the field level.

The analytical \(\Gamma\) derived according to equation () provides spectrally variant probability of photons reaching the sensor. The analytical \(\Gamma\) has different ranges at the two wavelengths \(\lambda\) and are in agreement with the ones shown in (Yang and Tol 2018). Typically, the escape probability is lower at 687 nm (Figure E) than at 760 nm (Figure F) because of the significant reabsorption and decreases with increase of LAI, while the escape probability at 760 nm is positively correlated with LAI due to higher chance of multiple recollisions. In contrast, the candidate VI (Figure C-D) have ranges which cannot be interpreted as percentage chance of photons escaping in observation direction and would need to be refactored to represent the real coefficient of escape probability. However, for the scaling approach used in this paper this is not necessary since the equation considers the ratio of the canopy structure among locations.The spatial pattern of NDVI and NIRv closely mirror our observations in the field. NIRv, being a transformation of NDVI appears to provide a sharper distinction. In other words it is less saturated in higher values.

17.2.2 Evaluation of predictive performance and error decomposition for the four candidate proxies

All models for \(SIF_{760}\) except the FCVI explain slightly more than half of the variability (R2 between 0.62 and 0.65). The error decomposition shows that all are centered around the 1:1 (\(SB \approx 0\)) (Figure ), while the dominant part of the error is due to lack of correlation (LC). Although NDVI outperformes all other proxies in terms of overal MSD it also has the largest slope of prediction (NU). This is a good example of the advantage in using the Gauch decomposition of total error of prediction. If only MSD would be considered NDVI is obviously the best predictior for \(SIF_{760}\), however, the decomposition of MSD reveales that it is outperformed by NIRv in rotation of the transformation. Minimizing the rotation must have high priority in our oppinion, since it suggest a stronger failure of prediction.

All models suffer from violation of indipendend and identically distributed (i.i.d.) residuals along the tails. The underprediction at higher and overprediction at lower values indicates the non-linear nature of the relationship between SIF and show that the selected proxies do not capture the process at very low and at very high canopy covers.

For \(SIF_{687}\) the analytical \(\Gamma\) outperforms the VI proxies. However, they all fail to correct for structural effects in the red region (\(SIF_{687}\)) yielding a weak R2. This is to be expected since SIF in the red region is less affected by canopy structure but rather by chlorophyll concentration and the strength of the signal in this region is masked by the levels of sensor noise.

17.2.3 Sensitivity to the selected reference location

In equation () the best reference scalar is found by minimizing the MSD. Figure () plots the denominator component (\(\Gamma_r\)) and figure () - the nominator component of the reference location. Figures () and () show the sensitivity of prediction diagnostics (MSD, SB and NU) to the selection of reference locations (\(\frac{SIF_r}{\Gamma_r}\)). We see that the method tunes mainly the bias estimate from the MSD decompositon, less so the slope between predicted and observed, and there is no effect whatsoever on the scatter (LC) (Figures () and () - LC was ommited from the sensitivy graphs). In fact, the LC is constant for any \(\Gamma\) since it is calculated from the data itself and does not depend on the position relative to the 1:1 line. The sensitivity was evaluated only for the \(SIF_{760}\) model.

Comparing the two graphs reveals that the nominator component (\(SIF_r\)) has a larger influence on MSD. MSD is mimized for \(SIF_r\) = 2.1 for all three VI proxies for canopy structure. Notice that MSD is less sensitive to changes in reference SSIF_r$ when NIRv is considered. Also, while for NDVI and FCVI proxies NU tends to deteriorate with larger SIF_r values (smaller NU is better) it improves for NIRv. Thus for NIRv is the only index which improves both the bias and the slope at higher values.

17.2.4 Spatial patterns of residuals

In all residual maps, highest deviations are observed along the edges of the field and in the less vegetated higher terrace as seen by the increased levels of local autocorrelation in these areas. In addition, the residuals for NDVI and FCVI have higher global spatial autocorrelation (\(I_{NDVI} = 0.86\) and \(I_{FCVI} = 0.90\)) than NIRv (\(I_{NIRv} = 0.78\)). Moran’s I closer to zero indicates less autocorrelation. The residual maps for the analytically derived \(\Gamma\) and NIRv are very similar with slightly less local autocorrelation observed for NIRv. Their surface in the lower and mid sections of the field appear smoother and the patchiness of the upper terrace is less visible than in the residual maps for NDVI and FCVI which is the expected outcome if the proxy for canopy structure is correct. However, the analytical \(\Gamma\) and NIRv still underperform in areas with low vegetation cover, along the edges, and in the higher terrace. For \(SIF_{687}\), the global trend along the elevation gradient is now translated into the residuals. In addition, there is emergence of new, unseen patterns in the mid section of the field. Considering that the NIRv still underperform at very high SIF values these patches might be indicative of the maximum SIF emitted in the field.

17.3 Discussion

17.3.1 On derivation of proxy estimates for \(\Gamma\)

In this paper we used the recently published analytical derivations for canopy scattering to define a theoretical proxy for canopy structural effects \(\Gamma\) on emmited SIF. We used reflectance data retrieved from an ariborne hyperspectral sensor and additional field measurements to parameterize the \(\Gamma\) and evaluate its performance. The range of the resulting spectrally variant \(\Gamma\) was similar to those published before. The factor was lower in the red and higher in the far red region. We tested the analytically derived \(\Gamma\) and three VIs for their ability to correct for canopy structure. Using an error decomposition apporach we evaluated the four proxies showing that they explain up to 65% of the variability in TOC SIF as measured by a low altitude airborne imaging spectrometer.

The advantage of deriving \(\Gamma\) using such analytical approach lies in the interpretability of the intermediate as well as the final results. For example, the \(\Gamma\) maps alone can be subject to interpretation and reveal surface and subsurface heteregoneity providing additional information about the field. The calculated analytical \(\Gamma\) using equation () has a very strong negative linear relationship with NIRv (graph not shown) which is also confirmed by the practically similar results between NIRv and the analytical proxy for \(\Gamma\). The latter was paramaterized by an exponential decay function in the denominator and a saturating function as a nominator, and both are dependent, albeit through different proxies, to the amount of green biomass as is the NIRv.

The VIs we used were previously tested on satellite data and showed promising results in tracking \(SIF_{760}\). However, at the spatial scale relevant for physiological inference, they did not fuly track the observed SIF. This proves the complex non-linear nature of SIF - plant traits relation at finer scales, and suggests that a significant factor is missing from our definition. Namely, more elaborate estimates which include leaf angle distribution, and physiology must be included to improve the accuracy of the model while keeping physiological interpretation possible. Alternatively, models from the machine learning domain can deal with the highly non-linear nature of the problem and be applied for better prediction. However, they remain a black box and are unsuitable for interrogative and inferencial goals and will likely not aid further insights into physiologists understanding of plant status in the field.

17.3.2 On the evaluation methodology and the results

In this paper we used the LUE model for SIF to test whether it is theoretically possible to predict the SIF for any location on a map given a single reference measurement of SIF and a spatially explicit estimate of canopy structure \(\Gamma\). We tested this hypothesis for a list of candidate proxies for \(\Gamma\) using a SIF map retrieved from an airborne sensor. This methodology can be used to provide a numerical evaluation of proxies for canopy structure, solve scaling questions from ground to space, or infer about variability in plant physiological status in the context of precision agriculture. In all cases, the valuable information lies in the variation not explained by the scaling approach. In addition, we defined an extension of this conceptual evaluation model into the spatiotemporal domain by considering the diurnal course of SIF for the reference location (e.g. as measured by tower based systems - In supplementary material).

The best reference scalar (\(\frac{SIF_r}{\Gamma_r}\)) for the rescaling of SIF was found by repeatedly pooling from a random set of reference locations and minimizing the mean squared deviation between modeled SIF (equation ) and observed SIF. This type of Monte-Carlo approach has the additional benefit of finding the central point of the joint probability density function (PDF) of \(\Gamma\) and SIF in the field. E.g. the best \(\Gamma\) will sit equidistant from the tails of these inputs. Analysis of this sort can be used to inform about the potential sampling locations for ground sampling of leaf level fluorescence measurements. For example, by sampling locations with different MSD scores (e.g. central MSD but also locations yielding positive or negative bias) one should cover the joint variability in SIF and \(\Gamma\) in the field and not only the more apparent variability of plant density. It must be noted that such application relies on the availability of at least one prior SIF map for the target area.

Our results confirm that NIRv outperforms NDVI in tracking canopy structure. Since NDVI is a proxy of light interception, and Rnir is good predictor of vegetation proportion, the scalar product of both is expected to be indicative of light interception by green vegetation (fPARleaf). The results of NIRv and the analytical \(\Gamma\) are strikingly similar, confirming the usability of NIRv as a correction factor for the SIF structural component and inference about true photosynthetic yield (\(\phi F\)). However, NIRv alone is probably not sufficient to account for all the nonlinearities and complexities occuring at the fine spatial scale of an individual agricultural field.

Since by definition any proxy will “approximate” the true canopy structure within certain error, an evaluation of the performance of the canopy structural parameter is needed. A numerical assessment procedure aiding the evaluation was outlined. The total error of prediction (e.g. the mean squared deviation) was decomposed into three easy to interpret sources - error contribution due to lack of correlation (LC), due to bias (SB), and due to rotation round the 1:1 line(NU). Each of those three has a intuitive interpretation and since their relative contribution to the total error sum up to unity, enables assessment of the source of error.

For the indices evaluated in this paper, the LC is by far the dominant source of error. The LC points to the existence and enumerates the levels of residual noise inside the system. The LC is in fact equivalent to the coefficient of determination (R2). The variation unexplained by the models might originates from three main sources. First, the assumption of constant \(\phi F\) inside the field is likely to be violated given the large variability in soil subsurface conditions and gradient in soil moisture. Second, approxiamation of \(\Gamma\) does not take explicitly the leaf angle distribution and can benefit from better parameterization. Third, the retrieval of SIF from large distances is subject of intensive research and can contain uncertainty. In fact, a preliminary analysis done on the data without applying any noise reduction yielded a significantly lower coefficient of determination in all cases.

A small proportion of the overal error (up to 10% in the case of NIRv and the analytical \(\Gamma\)) was atributed to rotation (NU). The contribution of NU to the overal error further suggests the omission of important explanatory factor. Indeed, we tested bending the shape of the present definition to its theoretical limits by NLS estimation for most of the parameter (extinction coefficient \(k_1\) and \(\alpha\)) and saw mild improvement in the middle and severe failure in the lower bounds of the value range. We hypothesized that the missing factor is either one of chlorophyll, leaf angular distribution, or physiology itself. Remember, that we have conviniently assumed spatially invariant physiology (\(\phi F\)) across the region.

Finally, a significant information is hidden in the spatial component of the residuals. Given a theoretical perfect proxy for the effect of canopy structure, the residuals would be without the influence of canopy structure in absolute SIF units. In such scenario, the spatial variation of the residual maps is indicative of differences in physiological status on the ground. A residual map closely mirroring the spatial patterns of SIF suggests that the proxy is inapropriate. In contrast, the emergence of new patterns, not seen in either inputs (SIF and \(\Gamma\) maps) can suggest the existense of a secondary underlying variability not related to structure. Thus, given a theoretical perfect proxy, the emergence of new patterns should be taken as a sign of possible violation of the assumption of constant physiology.

17.3.3 On possible application in the upscaling of SIF

A possible application of the location model (equation ) is the spatial upscaling of physiological and SIF observations from a fixed measurement point on the ground to a satellite data source with coarser spatial and spectra resolution. For example, Sentinel-2 (S2) imagery can be used as an intermediary step from which a biom/land cover specific definition for \(\Gamma\) is retrievable. Then, the validation measurements on the ground and S2 derived \(\Gamma\) at the measurement pixel form the reference pixel in the location model and can be used to scale the SIF for the entire S2 scene using equation () and fine tuned against the SIF products from FLEX.

17.3.4 Conclusion

The scaling strategy presented here has the potential to serve as a link between spatiotemporal sampling of SIF on the ground, e.g. using moving or stationary measuring systems, and larger scale remotely acquired SIF estimates which can cover larger areas but are frozen at the specific time of image acquisition (by considering the extendend spatiotemporal model in the supplementary materials). it can be used to spatially extrapolate fluorescence measurements from a single location to the surrounding region given a proxy for canopy structure is retrievable from a remote sensing data source. The tested proxies for canopy structure leave enough unexplained variability and underperform at the tails of the value range. Future work needs to focus on testing more complex definitions for \(\Gamma\) by including the effect of chlorophyll and canopy architecture, and on combining physiological data on the leaf level to constrain the fluorescence yield \(\phi F\), which is assumed to be spatially constant in this paper.
