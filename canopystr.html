Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 5 Canopy structure

In the chapter 2 we started by stating that incoming radiation can either be intercepted or transmitted without interaction. Plants have evolved in such a way as to optimised the interception of sunlight for photosynthesis in the photosythetically active region between 400 and 900 nm. The absorption itself happens thanks to the chlorophyll molecule, but the efficiency of this absorption, is governed by structural organisation of the light absorbing elements.

At a single leaf level the amount of intercepted light depends on the amount of the primary absorbing agent - the chlorophyll content. A secondary factor is the internal organisation of chlorophyplast structures. At the canopy scale, the leaf and canopy organisation is widely seen as an evolutionary tool for optimisation of light interception. The amount of leaves, their orientation are governed by the levels and direction of the radiation.

5.1 Measuring canopy structure

Measuring canopy structure on the ground is labor intensive. However, it is needed to estimate ground truth. There are two approaches to measure plant characteristics on the groud - destructive and non destructive approaches. Destructive methods typically yield better results especially when measuring leaf area or chemical composition. However, their destructive nature does not let repeated sampling and therefore are unsuitable for long term monitoring. In addition they tend to be the most labour intensive in comparison to the non-destructive.

The non-destructive methods, as the name suggest, does not destry the object and therefore are very suitable for long term monitoring. Typically they include some kind of sensor or measuring device from which can either measure the charactristic directly or after a recalculaton. For example, leaf angle can be measure directly by angle meter or estimated from stereo imagery. A typical estimation for leaf are is calculated as the ratio between the radiation levels above and bellow the canopy.

5.2 modeling canopy structure

Radiative transfer models have defined faith of light inside the canopy. Depending on their complexity they are 1D, (???), 3D model.s With increasing complexity of the model, the definition of canopy structure also increases. In the 1D and 2D models canopy structure is defined by several parameters - namely LAI and parameter for leaf angle distribution. AT 3D models the definition of each individual leaf element is possible.

5.3 Retrieval of canopy structure

The state of the vegetation can be inferred from spectral reflectance information. One of the first adopted methods was to calculate the ratio of spectral reflectance in the red and near infrared regions. Such simple spectral indices based on broadband multispectral sensors are used ever since first Earth observation missions later known as Landsat started delivering data.

The increasing availability of hyperspectral data, and the increase of computation power prompted development of more complex methods which utilizes the whole part of the spectrum for information extraction. Generally, approaches to estimate vegetation status from multi- or hyperspectral reflectance can be summarized into four groups (for review Malenovsky):

  • building empirical relationship (parametric approaches),
  • inversion of analytical models (mechanistic approaches),
  • using machine learning (non-parametric approaches),
  • using Look-up table (LUTs) or hybrid approaches.

The first group, the parametric approaches seek to build robust empirical models between spectra and observed vegetation parameters. A drawback of parametric methods is their strong assumptions on the input data. For example, independence of input variables. Since the feature space (number of spectral bands) is intrinsically correlated (e.g.reflectance at 450nm can be predicted from reflectance at 451 nm ) often dimensionality reduction must be applied. One advantage of dimensionality reduction is that the new features (principal components, spectral end members) can be easily interpreted. For example, the first principal component of a remote sensing image corresponds to the overall brightness in the scene while the second principal component is typically a proxy for the colouring (e.g. greenness if the image represents predominantly natural area).

One big advantage of non-parametric methods such as machine learning is that they pose little prior assumptions about the data. Relaxing the prior assumptions is an advantage when working with spectral data with unknown noise, or with test and validation sample with unkown measurement error. Especially if sufficient ammount of data is available, non parametric methods can be very powerful in extracting information from messy data. One prominent drawback is the potential overfiting to the training dataset which can lead to underperformance when presented with a novel dataset (e.g. from a different habitat). Therefore, they can be useful for prediction but not for understanding.

The mechanistic models are built on well-established physical principals of light propagation in space and light-vegetation interaction. These models are well suited for seeking understanding of the underlying processes. For example, through a sensitivity analysis. The advantage is that these radiative transfer models (RTMs) simulate a closed system, which enables inference on the specific processes (e.g. global sensitivity analysis verrelst). The main constrain is the high computational cost especially with more complex models, such as 3D models, ray tracing models, or combined radiative transfer and biochemical models.

The hybrid methods, seek to combine the analytical robustness of the mechanistically defined models while reducing their computational burden. This is acomplished by taking advantage of parametric or non-parametric approaches. This is where the LUT table approach becomes useful.

5.3.1 Look-up Tables

In the Look-up Table (LUT) approach mechanistically defined radiative transfer models (RTM) are used to simulate (predict) reflectance R (or bi-directional reflectance factor BRF(λ)) for range of biophysical parameters (n). The resulting combination of biophysical parameters (n) and spectra (BRF) are stored in a database table (a LUT) to be interrogated later (Figure 5.1). The advantage is reduced computational speed and interpretability.

Figure 5.1: Schematic overview of the RTM inversion procedure using Look-up Table (LUT). An RTM is used to forward simulate reflectance. A cost function is used to find the best fit between observed and simulated spectra. The result is used to predict biophysical parameters from a new data set such as hyperspectral maps.

Choosing the right complexity of the initial RTM depends on the spatial scale, resolution, and the diversity and size of validation data at hand (the parameter space N). 1D and 2D models have less parameters (lower number of n ) making them easy to parameterize. Therefore, simpler models are more suitable for regional and global spatial scales and larger temporal scales (e.g. seasonal patterns). In contrast, complex 3D models, such as ray tracing models, can be used to study closely linked processes at proximal scales (e.g. drone, or phenotyping, but also smart agriculture application). Having a-priori knowledge of the boundaries of the parameter space can reduce the size of the LUT. This improves results and lowers processing time. To find the unknown biophysical parameters of a given a new set (R*) of spectral signatures, say from remote sensing data, the LUT table is interrogated. The closest match between the reflectance R from the LUT table and the new observed reflectance is found by minimizing some kind of “closeness” estimate. This closeness estimate is known as cost function. Other terms with the same meaning include: distance, statistical distance divergence, or metric.

5.3.2 Cost functions

The cost function for the simplest case, say a linear model, is some kind of least square estimate (LSE) which can be found numerically (e.g. \(min(observed^2 – measured^2)\)). However, such parametric models have certain strong assumptions such as identically and independent distribution (i.i.d.) of residuals and errors, and noise. If i.i.d. can be assumed then LSE estimates can be used and must yield robust results. However, in cases when noise is not normal with mean 0 LSE results can be misleading. Therefore, different other optimisation methods (cost function definitions) are implemented which can relax some of the assumption from LSE. The cost functions can be grouped into three categories Leonenko:

  1. Information measures
  2. M-estimates
  3. Minimum contrast methods.

The cost functions falling in the first group - the information measures, seek to minimize the distances between two probability distributions. Information measures include divergence, distance, or entropy based measures. The second class, M-estimates, is a broad class of functions in which probability density is not considered. (e.g. least squares optimization). The third class, the minimum contrast estimates, express BRF as a spectral density function, and minimize the distance between the observed and predicted spectral densities, as oppose to the probability density used in the information measures.

A list of cost functions was summarized in Leonenko and implemented in ARTMO (verrels). Some consideration when choosing the cost function are the distribution of noise contained in the data, the sample size, sample bias, other assumptions. In most cases it is advisable to test several distance functions and choose an optimal performer based on predefined criteria. We will come back to this idea in chapter LAI inversion EDA, where we will evaluate the performance of 14 cost functions, and choose the best performer to predict LAI.

5.4 The effect of canopy structure on SIF

As of this writing there is a significant body of work directly or indirectly discussing the issue of canopy structural effects. The question of canopy structure can be addressed either mechanistically through analytical models or empirically - through experimental data. The former category includes sensitivity analysis studies, or analytical derivations of scaling coefficients. The latter group includes dedicated experimental design with artificially induced or naturally occuring variability of canopy structure and the subsequent measurement of TOC SIF.

The sensitivity analysis is a way to evaluate the strength with which a variation in model input parameters (canopy structure in our case ) affects the output of a model (TOC SIF). The general approach includes a simulation of TOC fluorescence for range of canopy structural parameters and calculation of a numerical estimate of the sensitivity for each input. Sobol’s variation is one such esitmate. A global sensitivity analysis study of SCOPE model showed that more than 90% of the variability of TOC SIF can be explained by four input parameters alone. Three of those parameters (LAI, leaf angle distribution and Cab) are canopy structural parameters and only one parameter - Vcmax is related to physiology.

The sensitivity analysis approach gives further opportunity to not only establish the strength of the relationship but to examine its shape. For example, (Rhoul et al. 2014) used FluorSAIL canopy radiative transfer model simulations to study the effect of different structural parameters on the outgoing F signal. Results showed significant diurnal variation of SIF due to LAI and leaf inclination, with higher LAI values generally diminishing the F diurnal variation. Many other papers have dedicated simulation chapter and examination of the shape of input output parameter space. Bellow is plot of a SCOPE simulation relating the change of LAI and chlorophyll content to total hemispherically integrated fluorescence.

Figure 5.2: SCOPE simulation of the effect of leaf area index and chlorophyll content on hemispherically integrated fluorescence signal in the O2A region.

The effect of canopy structure on SIF signal can be studied emprically. In these cases data is collected over a controlled experimental setup, or a naturally occuring structural differences are used as a quasi experiment or “natural” experiment. An example of the former is a leaf cutting experiment performed in corn (R. Colombo, Meroni, and Rossini 2016) showing that fluorescence and apparent fluorescence yield (Fy derived by normalization FTOC with radiance at 750 nm) are covarying with Leaf area Index (LAI). After dividing SIF by simple ratio (SR) spectral vegetation index (VI) the relationship to LAI is lost. The study suggests that it should be possible to construct fluorescence indices insensitive to canopy structure.
