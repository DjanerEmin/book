Chapter 7 Study area

7.1 Location

Selhausen study area is located in North Rhine-Westphalia province of Germany between the cities of Aachen and Cologne (50o52’09“N, 6o27’01”E). The site is part of the Tereno network of environmental observatory infrastructure and, therefore, an object of intensive research. One aspect of this research is the annual overflight by a hyperspectral sensor specifically designed for SIF retrieval (HyPlant) (Section 3.4.2).

The flight pattern consists of 7 flight lines.The combined mosaic comprises an elongated rectangular scene with a width (across flight direction) of aprrox. 2 km, and length (along flight direction) - 14 km. The boundaries of the mosaic can be roughly defined by the main roads of the German road network. The Highway A4 to the South, L264 to the East, and B56 to the West. To the north the flight pattern extends to the Hambach Forest with FZ Juelich. The overflown area has an approximate size of about 27 km2 and is predominantly used for agriculture with cereals and sugar beet making up the largest proportion of crops grown there.

7.2 Climate

The climate in the region is typical oceanic (Cfb according to Köppen classification) with mean annual air temperature around 10 oC and mean annual precipitation slightly above the country average - 700 mm. The 30 year average climatogram shows a relatively low variation of monthly mean precipitation (with a minimum of 60 mm/month in April and a maximum - slightly above 80 mm/month in June) and typical for oceanic climate mild winter and summer. The maximum monthly temperature is bellow 20 oC in July (Figure 7.2).

Figure 7.2: Climate for the region of Selhausen study area. Historical data 1981 - 2010 Aachen downloaded from the German National Meteorological Service https://www.dwd.de/

7.3 Land use

The land is used overwhelmingly for agriculture. The arable land is partitioned into medium to large fields (the median field size is 1.3 ha). The residential land use (which also includes a small proportion of industrial areas) is the second largest, but still, it is dwarfed by the extensive proportion of agricultural fields. Some small fields are reserved as meadows mainly around settlements, and areas associated with horse or cattle ranches (Figure 7.3).

Figure 7.3: Land use map

The forest proportion in the area is very small - it consists of the southern edge of Hambach (excluded from the statistic here) and few smaller tree patches and hedges interjecting the agricultural landscape. Hedges are preserved mainly along the water retention ditches. New effort for recreation of hedges are observed in the past several years but the trees are still too young (Figure 7.4).

Figure 7.4: Distribution of the main land use classes in Selhausen study area.

The crops grown in the region reflect the market incentives in the region and the climate predisposition. The most commonly sown crop group is the cereals. At a second place is sugar beet which is in demand from the local sugar industry. A small proportion of the fields, mainly managed by a single farming company, is used for vegetable farming. The energy (alternative biomass) and forage crops are the least common crop. The agricultural practice is fully mechanized and employs relatively small number of mainly seasonal workers (mainly for vegetable harvesting). Rain irrigation is used in the potato and the vegetable fields. Harvesting of cereals typically occurs in July, corn harvesting follows in September, and beet is typically harvested in November. Rotational crops are sawn to improve soil nutrients.
