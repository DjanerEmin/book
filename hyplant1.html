Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 13 Exploratory analysis of HyPlant data

The main goal of this chapter is to introduce the HyPlant data structure and perform an exploratory data analysis of the low altitude flight pattern over Selhausen. The design and characteristics of HyPlant were discussed in Section 3.4.2.

13.1 HyPlant Data

HyPlant consist of two sensing modules - a dual-channel broad band imager in the VIS and NIR spectrum (380-2500 nm) with 622 spectral bands (DUAL), and a second, high resolution imaging spectrometer covering the red and NIR regions (670-780 nm) specifically designed for fluorescence retrieval (FLUO) (Rascher et al. 2015).

The low altitude flight pattern over the Selhause research site consists of 7 flightlines. The nominal ground sampling distance (the pixel resolution) is 1 meter. We will focus on the sugar beet fields (shown in red color in figure 13.1). Sugar beet was the predominant green crop at time of data acquisition. To study the within field variability we will focus on the small subset of fields in the center of the low altitude mosaic (Figure 13.1).

Figure 13.1: Overview map of the area covered by the low altitude flight of HyPlant. Sugar beet fields are shown in red. The small area for pixel based analysis is marked with a rectangle.

Bellow are maps of the two retrieved SIF estimates and plots showing the distribution of SIF values for both retrieval regions (O2A and O2B) within the images. The four vegetation fields in center of the frame stand out with higher values (in green colours). The surrounding fields are bare soil at time of image acqusition (in purple colours). The fourth field to the East is a potato field (Figure 13.2).

Figure 13.2: Maps of SIF in O2A and O2B bands and corresponding histogram distribution.

The shape of the histogram distribution of SIF retrieved at 760 nm (O2A, top graph) resembles a bimodal normal distribution with one mode centered around 0 and a second - centered around 3. This corresponds to the two classes of ground cover observed in this scene - soil and vegetation cover, respectively. The bimodality is not obvious in the case of SIF retrieved at 687 nm (O2A).

Figure 13.3: Maps of SIF in O2A and O2B bands and corresponding histogram distribution.

When we mask out the non sugar beet fields we can observe that the distribution of SIF retrieved at 760 nm becomes unimodal. However, there are still a small number of negative values (Figure 13.3). These are most likely soil pixels somewhere along the borders of the fields. We will visualising those negative pixels next. A more detailed outlier detection and quality criteria were defined in the master thesis of Vera Krieger (2019)

13.2 Outlier detection

In order to visualise the negative values in both SIF images, we will reclassify the image into two categories - pixels bellow and above 0. The plots bellow show the spatial distribution of negative SIF values (Figures 13.4 and 13.5).

\(SIF ratio = SIF687/SIF760\)

Figure 13.4: Spatial distribution of negative values for SIF in O2A retrieval region.

Figure 13.5: Spatial distribution of negative values for SIF in O2B retrieval region.

As expected, negative values are constrained mainly along the borders of the fields or along tractor tracks. In all analysis to follow, negative values will be masked out.

Finally, a look a the distribution of SIF ratio will be shown. SIF ratio was calculated with the larger SIF 760 as denominator to constrain the majority of the values to the range between 0 and 1.

## -415.4913682    0.2142609    0.2619753    0.2941172    0.3237527    0.3525581    0.3838051    0.4208344    0.4703381    0.5575466 
##         100% 
##  107.2346857

The quantile distribution above shows that the majority (somewhere between 90% and 100%) of SIF ratio values are between 0 and 1. Obviously, negative SIF ratio values are possible if one and only one of the SIF estimates is bellow zero. On the other hand, unrealistically high SIF ratio values (SIF ratio larger than say 2) point to a very small denominator (SIF 760). This is possible when the vegetation cover of a pixel is scarce (e.g. a soil pixel) or plants are approaching senescence (as seen in figure 11.6). Bellow is a plot of SIF ratio pixel with values above 2 (Figure 13.6).

Figure 13.6: Spatial distribution of negative values for SIF ratio.

Based on the findings above, an outlier removal inside all sugar beet fields was conduced following three exclusion criteria:

  1. Simple Ratio VI values higher than 40 were excluded ( a 40 fold difference in NIR/Red reflection is already highly unlikely).

  2. Negative SIF values were excluded as they are by definition not possible.

  3. SIF ratio values above 2 was excluded since SIF in O2A band (the denominator in SIF ratio) is mostly higher than in O2B band (nominator)

Applying The three criteria above resulted in exclusion of approx. 3% of pixels from the sugar beet pixels, the most being attributed to negative SIF values (Figures 13.7 and 13.8).

Figure 13.7: Spatial and histogram distributions of SIF values after excluding outliers.

Figure 13.8: Spatial and histogram distributions of SIF ratio after excluding outliers.

13.3.4.1 Difference in means

The Student’s two sample unpaired t-test is test is sensitive to sample size. with increasing sample size it is more likely to confirm a difference in means. The appropriate size of the sample is the subject of branch of statistical science called Power Analysis. In order to decrease the power of the sample size has on the statistical test it is advisable to perform a bootstrap replicate with smaller portion of the data (Table ??).

The bootstrap replication of the T-test shows that there is no significant difference between average terrace values for the Vegetation Indices and for SIF in O2B band. In fact, the only significant difference in means (very highly significant, p < 0.005) is for SIF in O2A band and for elevation.

13.3.6 Scale dependence

Choosing the appropriate scale at which to study a particular phenomenon is important. The shape or the strenght of a relationship beteween two environmental variables might differ when observed at two different scales (see theoretical section ??). Identifying the scale of relevance (the appropraite operational scale) can be done empirically by comparing some goodness-of-fit statistics across the possible scales. In the next chapter we will perform analysis on two different operational scales. The agrosystem scale which we will define in the next chapter takes the individual agricultural field as an elementary sampling unit. However, unlike raster pixels, the agricultural fields have different size (Figure 13.16). This requires a preliminary analysis to see wether the size of the field has any impact on the values of our inputs.

Figure 13.16: Histogram distribution of field size in Selhausen

Figure 13.17 bellow plots the most important distribution characteristics as a function of field size. What we see is that there is no significant effect of field size on the the distribution of SIF values itself. Most importantly, the standard deviation (in other words the variability) of SIF is scale invariant.
