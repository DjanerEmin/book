Chapter 14 Retrieval of LAI and Cab from HyPlant Reflectance

This chapter shows the results from the Look-up Table (LUT) methodology used to retrieve LAI from HyPlant images. A combination of leaf level (Prospect) and canopy level (4SAIL) radiative transfer model (RTM) was used to forward simulate reflectance for a range of input canopy biophysical parameters. PROSAIL was chosen for its simplicity in parameterisation, interpretability, and wide use.

The resulting hyperspectral library was fitted to a small set of ground truth measurements using 14 different cost functions representative of the 3 cost functions families described in (???) (discussed in section ??). A limited number of LAI measurements on the ground were used for training. No validation was possible due to the small amount of ground truth data.

14.1 Materials

14.1.1 HyPlant

A research aircraft carying a hi-resolution hyperspectral sensor (HyPlant - Section 3.4.2) on board was flown over the Selhausen research area on 19.07.2016 at noon. Hyperspectral data in the range between 400 and 2500 recorded using DUAL was used to derive plant biophysical and structural parameters. In addition, the regions with strong water absorption were removed from the spectrum after (???)

14.1.2 Study Area

The Selhausen study area is predominantly mixed agricultural landscape (Chapter 14.1.2). In total 59 field with sugar beet were identified Through a supervised approach, which included a large scale land cover classification (???) and visual interpretation of visible and false color images.

14.1.3 Ground truth

The values of LAI on the ground were collected through two distinct measurement procedures in two nearby fields. The first data set consist of seasonal non-destructive measurements using Sunscan. The second approach harvested the above ground biomass at 1 m2 and estimated leaf area in the lab using LI-cor LA meter.

Both samples are part of a larger dataset collected as a part of joint environmental data gathering effort in the framework of activities within the Transregional Collaborative research Center. The full data set was used to predict seasonal patterns of LAI from Sentinel-2 images in (???).

For the purposes of this chapter, the ground dataset was restricted to the nearest date to the HyPlant overpass. In addition, after a preliminary exploratory LUT-inversions, the LAI destructive measurement were seen as more reliable and robust estimators of LAI. Albeit with only 6 replications these ground truth observations were used to test performance of 13 cost functions, from which later 3 were selected to retrieve spatially explicit LAI in the area of Selhausen.

LUT was generated using Prospect 4 â€“ SAIL combination. This was a test case so we kept the size of the LUT to minimum to increase processing time. ProSAIL simulation was performed for range of LAI and Cab parameters while other parameters were kept constant. This was done in order to minimize the processing time givven the large study area, and circumvene the ill-posedenes of such inversion procedures (Table 14.1).

14.1.5 Cost function selection

We tested the performance of 13 cost functions (Table ??). Inversion procedure included the following regularisation options. First, an addition of noise to spectrum in the range between 1 and 15% to minimize ill-posed effect. Second, spectral fit was evaluated for an average from 1-15% of the best solitions. All procedures were implemented in ARTMO (???).

14.2.1 Cost function evaluation table

There are a variety of goodness-of-fit measures in statistical domain. Some of the most common can be interpreted as follows (???):

  • RMSE (error index) indicates the magnitude of error in variable units.
  • R^2$ (correlation based) reflects spatial and temporal patters but does not measure actual difference.
  • NRMSE (dimensionless in %) indicates model relative error in %. Suitable for comparison between variables with different value ranges.
  • NSE (dimensionless index) indicates the predictive power. It tells if model performs at least as accurate as the mean of observed data.

Table ?? presents the performance of each of the functions as measured by several goodness-of-fit statistics. The table can be interogated and rearanged in ascending or descending order based on a column (in the online version of this work).

Both RMSE and Exponential cost functions yield the best and identical performance (e.g.Â find the same mimimum). Nevertheless, RMSE is close to 1 unit of LAI which translates to about 20% variability (in terms of NRMSE). In general, Higher R2 is also associated with higher predictive power (NSE) but NSE values drop more drastically, than R2 value do. In fact, while 9 (nine) cost functions have R2 higher than 0.5, only four cost functions have a better than chance predictive performance (NSE > 0).This suggest that R2 and RMSE alone can in some cases misslead the decision of which cost function to be used.
