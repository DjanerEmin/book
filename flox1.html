Exploring the effect of canopy structure on top-of-canopy sun-induced fluorescence

Chapter 10 Description of FLOX device and exploratory data analysis of diurnal measurements

Retrieval of Sun-induced Fluorescence (SIF) in field conditions requires highly resolved spectral information to be available in the SIF emittance range between 600 and 800 nm. Such spectral resolution is needed to accurately identify the width and depth of the oxygen absorption features used in the iFLD method (M. Meroni et al. 2009). Currently on the market there are several commercially available spectroradiometers which answer this criteria. (Table 1 in supplementary material in (Aasen et al. 2019)). Comparative studies showed that high spectral resolution and sufficient SNR are essential for the retrieval of fluorescence(Julitta et al. 2016).

The design of any SIF measurement device is governed by the particular use but generally includes high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) spectrometer, optical fibers, CPU controller, data logger, protective housing, and cooling chamber. There are few considerations which are design relevant. Notably, the total incoming radiation can be recorded either via a reflectance panel, or a separate optical fiber. In the former case, the design must include a rotational device which alternates the fiber between the target vegetation and the reference panel (as in (???)). In the latter case, an alternator (a shutter or splitter device) is installed between two fibers and the spectrometer, as in (???), and the two fibers are permanently installed in upward and downward direction for almost simultaneus acquistion.

SIF is fairly small proportion of the total recorded signal, thus, all stages of the lifecycle of the device (e.g. design, production, maintanance, and deployment) must ensure good data quality. For example, it is crucial to maintain stable temperature within the system since the recorded currents are sensitive to the the slightest changes in temperature. This gives rise to the need to track housing and sensor temeparature for the purpose of ad/hoc quality assurance of the recorded data.

Finally, even a well designed system can still be influenced by external factors such as the selection of measurement targets and the installation process. While the exact target is governed by the specific scientific question there are several general installation considerations. First, to minimize the anisotropic effects all fibers must be accurately leveled at 90o angle. Second, to minimize artefacts due to shadows the fibers must be installed to the south facing side of the tower (for the nordern hemisphere).

In this chapter we will present the design and perform a quality assesment of a commercially available spectrometer device, which was developed to retrieve unatained long term measurements of SIF and broadband reflectance. Then, we will explore two 3-day diurnal datasets taken during the summer of 2018 - one over sugar beet and one over winter wheat. The data was acquired with FLOX device (S/N:JB-014-UR) in the area of Selhausen when both canopies were fully developed but before signs of senescence. The winter wheat measurements were acquired on 6th (half day), 7th, and 8th of May 2018 from distance 3.5 meters above the ground.1 The sugar beet measurements were acquired on 1st, 2nd, and 3rd July at a slightly lower distance above the ground - 2.2 meters. Every day the location inside the field was changed to account for spatial heterogeneity.

10.1 FLOX description and metadata

FLOX is an automated field spectroscopy device designed to collect unattended, continuous, long-term hyperspectral measurements. The body of the device is a waterproof PELI case (model 1535) with power outlet, optic fiber outlet, and cooling fans built in on the sides. Inside the box, a cooling chamber houses two high resolution spectrometers (Ocean Optics Inc. USA). A high resolution QEPro spectrometer is programmed in the range between 650 and 800 nm with high radiometric resolution to enable the passive retrieval of fluorescence. The second spectrometer is programmed in the VIS and NIR spectral range (Table ??). Each spectrometer is connected to two optical fibers via alternating module - one with 25o foreoptics for measuring reflected radiation, and second equiped with a diffuser ment to measure the total incoming radiation.

The design of FLOX allows for the near simultaneous measurement of solar irradiance and reflected radiance. One elementary measurement cycle consist of a sequential acquisition of dark current, incoming radiation (from the upward looking), reflected radiation, second incoming radiation, and a second dark current. This procedure is repeated consecutively for both spectrometers and raw digital numbers are stored on an SD card in csv format. Integration time is automatically set at the beginning of each cycle. With the abovementioned settings one measurement cycle lasts between 20-30 seconds depending on the incoming light intensity.

10.3.2 Stability of SIF measurements

10.3.2.1 E-stability

The FLOX internally optimizes integration time at the start of a measurement cycle. The processing pipeline provides an estimate for the stability of the incomming radiation during one cycle. It is calculated as the percentage difference between the first and the second upward reading of incomming radiation. Put simply, it measures the change in light intensity within one single observation (e.g. within several seconds) (Table 10.5)

Table 10.5: Estimates of stability of ambient light calcualted from FLOX processing pipeline.
Field name Description
E_stability [%] Percentage difference between WR1 and WR2. Fluo range
E_stability full [%] Percentage difference between WR1 and WR2. Full range

In fact, under clear sky conditions, changes in light intensity within single observation are miniscule so that 99 % of all observations have an E-stability value of 0.65 % or less. The tail of the cumulative distribution for E-stability (the 5% of highest values) is :

This can be visualised with a plot (Figure 10.12):

Figure 10.12: Quantile distribution of stability of Incomming radiation within measurement cycle.

Clearly the top 1% contains all the outliers. As a rule of thumb, observations with an E-stability values under 1 % should be considered to yield a robust SIF estimates. Therefore, a next filtering step is to eliminate meausurements with E-stability value greater than 1%. However, it is also a good approach to check the cumulative frequency distribution of this estimate, as it can inform to the proportion of bad readings. Here, a confusion may arise from the fact that both E-stability and the quantile distribution are in percent. The interpretation of the graph above is that 99% of observations have an E-stability value of 0.65% or lower. By exluding E-stability > 1% we are excluding the far right end of the quantile distirbution which in this case is less than 1% of all observations.

Another way of investigating the effect of E-stability was shown in the supplementary materials for (Aasen et al. 2019). It plots the extinction of the relative standard deviation (RSD %) as a function of exclusion threshold. The authors do not explicitly state whether the RSD is calculated over the whole day or is a running window average. Here we will calculate local RSD considering a 5 minute window (a neighbourhood RSD) and compare it to the corersponding neighbourhood E-stability. We will use a diurnal dataset collected on 04.06.2018 in the same winter wheat field during partly cloudy weather (Figure 10.13).

Figure 10.13: One day of SIF measurements during a partly cluody weather. The appearence of clouds during midday is visible by the noise in SIF760.

In figure 10.13 above we saw how much the clouds affect the SIF signal. Particularly the SIF in O2A band is very sensitive to the ammount of incomming radiation. Now we will estimate the level of noise this adds to the data by calculating the 5 minute average relative standard deviation (RSD %) (Figure 10.14 and 10.15).

Figure 10.14: Diurnal RSD for a 5 minute window over one day (04.06.2018). Winter Wheat.

Finally, it must be noted that using E-stability alone as filtering mechanism for bad data might not yield perfect results. As seen from the graph above, considering E_stability alone will not exclude a stable but cloudy condition which can appear in the data as some larger cloud system moves in for significantly long time over the area. By considering a local neighbourhood of measurements we can improve the filtering process. 10.3.2.2 Negative SIF values

There are negative readigs for SIF in O2B (iFLD) (only one value of these is negative for SFM). We will exclude these. Such values are linked to physical disturbance of the measurement setup. For example, they may occure as a result of obstruction of the field of view or interference with the optic fibers.

10.4 Discussion

One big advantage of the SIF is its scalability from proximal to larger distances. Typically, validation stations are equipped with point spectrometers such as the one presented in this chapter with a footprint depending on the installation height. However, a singular point is not enough to be used as a validation for larger spatial resolutions such as the one expected to be delivered by the FLEX satellite. An additional spatially explicit sampling of sif can be used to extend the footprint of the ground validation stations, represent better the variability of the surrounding conditions, and include information on other land cover classes falling inside a single FLEX pixel, in effect aiding a more robust upscaling of SIF from the ground to the satellite. However, designing and implementing measurement devices for stationary long term use is made easy by the static nature of the measurement. In contrast, moving handheld devices will be more difficult to standardize and data from them will be inevitably noisier. In the next chapter we will use the FLOX device presented here to sample the spatial variability of SIF,and discuss the advantages and disadvantages of such a mobile meausurement system.
